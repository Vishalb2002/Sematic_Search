Abstract
Hand gesture recognition (HGR) is the most important part of human-computer interaction
(HCI). Static hand gesture recognition is equivalent to the classification of hand gesture
images. At present, the classification of hand gesture images mainly uses the Convolutional
Neural Network (CNN) method. The Vision Transformer architecture (ViT) proposes not
to use the convolutional layers at all but to use the multi-head attention mechanism to learn
global information. Therefore, this paper proposes a static hand gesture recognition method
based on the Vision Transformer. This paper uses a self-made dataset and two publicly avail-
able American Sign Language (ASL) datasets to train and evaluate the ViT architecture.
Using the depth information provided by the Microsoft Kinect camera to capture the hand
gesture images and filter the background, then use the eight-connected discrimination algo-
rithm and the distance transformation algorithm to remove the redundant arm information.
The resulting images constitute a self-made dataset. At the same time, this paper studies the
impact of several data augmentation strategies on recognition performance. This paper uses
accuracy, F1 score, recall, and precision as evaluation metrics. Finally, the validation accu-
racy of the proposed model on the three datasets achieves 99.44%, 99.37%, and 96.53%,
respectively, and the results obtained are better than those obtained by some CNN structures.

1 Introduction
With the rapid development of artificial intelligence and computer vision, human-computer
interaction has increased extensively in recent decades. Hand gesture information is more
accessible than sounds, brain electrical signals, and other information, so hand gesture
recognition has become one of the principal directions of human-computer interaction.
Hand gesture recognition usually is divided into static hand gesture recognition and dynamic
hand gesture recognition. There are two main methods for static hand gesture recogni-
tion. The first is hand gesture recognition based on wearable devices, such as data gloves
[23] and electromyography [35]. However, it has considerable inconvenience and high
cost.
In contrast, hand gesture recognition based on computer vision is more convenient. It
only needs to use the camera to obtain hand gesture images without wearing additional
equipment. The vision-based hand gesture recognition methods can divide into hand-crafted
and deep learning methods. Hand-crafted methods include complex visual processing, such
as hand-crafted feature extraction methods and classification algorithms. However, deep
learning methods use supervised learning to adapt to more diverse and complex challenges.
Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) is one of the deep learning methods and the most
important one. As a result, most of the current papers involving hand gesture recognition use
CNN-based model structures [2, 33, 37, 38, 42], such as AlexNet [20], VGG-16, VGG-19
[40], GoogLeNet [41], ResNet-50 [15].
At the same time, a new type of deep learning model used in natural language processing
(NLP) was proposed, which is called the Transformer. Furthermore, it has appeared in some
popularity in computer vision, such as image classification [14] tasks. Initially, it was intro-
duced to solve machine translation [49], language modelling [11] and speech recognition
[8]. And it successfully replaced the use of recurrent models (such as RNNs, LSTMs) in pro-
cessing sequence data. The key to the Transformer’s success is to rely on a mechanism called
self-attention, which can capture the internal correlation of data and effectively solve the
problem of long-distance dependence. In visual tasks, images are often processed; however,
the number of pixels in the image is much larger than the number of words in the sequence
task, so the attention mechanism is limited in the visual task. Hu et al. [16] proposed chan-
nel attention and SENet for this purpose. The core of SENet is a squeeze-and-excitation
(SE) block to collect global information, capture channel relationships, and improve repre-
sentation. To increase the receptive field, Wang et al. [50] proposed a non-local block and
introduced self-attention to computer vision. Huang et al. [17] proposed CCNet, in which
the self-attention operation is regarded as graph convolution, and the densely-connected
graph processed by self-attention is replaced by several sparsely-connected graphs. To this
end, it proposes criss-cross attention, which considers row attention and column attention
recurrently to obtain global information. Recently, a model called the Vision Transformer
(ViT) [14] was proposed by Dosovitskiy et al. It divides the image into multiple patches
to be input to the encoder module of the attention model, and there are minor changes
to the Transformer model, and this is a groundbreaking method. It is trained on a large
amount of data and computing resources, and the ViT architecture has been proved to
be more accurate in image classification tasks than the CNN architecture with the same
parameters [14].

Much research shows that under the premise of using enough data for training, the per-
formance of the ViT architecture is better than that of the CNN architecture in many aspects.
Firstly, under the premise of pre-trained on the ImageNet dataset and having the same error
rate, the ViT architecture has better transferability to downstream tasks than the CNN archi-
tecture [53]. Secondly, the ViT architecture has strong robustness under severe occlusions,
disturbances, and domain shifts compared with the CNN architecture [6, 28, 32].
This paper aims to train a classifier that can accurately recognize static hand gesture
images to complete the recognition of static hand gestures. Because the ViT structure has a
better effect on image classification than the CNN structure, the transferability and robust-
ness of the ViT structure on downstream tasks are better than the CNN structure. Therefore,
this paper recognizes static hand gestures based on the pre-trained ViT structure. This paper
applies the data augmentation strategies of the CutOut and the CutMix to improve the recog-
nition accuracy. In the end, under the three hand gesture datasets, the results obtained by
applying the ViT structure of this paper are better than most of the results obtained by
applying the CNN structures.
The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. In Section 2, some recent related
works on gesture recognition are summarized. The methods proposed in this paper mainly
include foreground segmentation, arm removal, the Vision Transformer architecture, and
data augmentation strategies, which are all described in Section 3. Section 4 introduces the
entire experiment on this paper and the experimental results on three datasets. Then finally,
Section 5 shows the conclusion of this paper.
2 Related works
The research methods of hand gesture recognition are roughly divided into methods based
on data gloves and electromyography, methods based on hand-crafted feature extraction,
methods based on computer vision, and combinations based on the three methods. The
following describes the recent related work on hand gesture recognition.
In the methods based on hand-crafted feature extraction, Pan et al. [31] proposed to use
Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) to segment the hand region of RGB images. Then the
segmented images were processed by Fourier Descriptor, Hu moments and Scale-invariant
feature transform (SIFT) to extract independent features. Before being classified by Support
Vector Machine (SVM), PCA and LDA were used to trim the dimensionality of the fea-
ture vector. Among the many dynamic hand gesture recognition methods, Bendarkar et al.
[3] proposed a real-time gesture recognition system based on a combination of static and
dynamic hand gestures based on convolutional neural networks (CNN). In the dynamic hand
gesture recognition part, a bidirectional ConvLSTM and 3DCNN were combined to form a
3DCNN-LSTM model.
Oyedotun and Khashman [29] proposed three different CNNs and SDAEs architectures
and applied them to Thomas Moeslund’s gesture recognition dataset for evaluation. After
the final training, the accuracy of CNN1 and SDAE3 on the test data achieved 91.33% and
92.83%, respectively.
Chevtchenko et al. [10] proposed a real-time hand gesture recognition architecture based
on feature fusion convolutional neural network (FFCNN), which combined Convolutional
Neural Network (CNN) and Gabor filters for feature extraction and gesture classification,
and the Tree-of-Parzen-Estimators (TPE) algorithm was used to search the hyperparam-
eters of the network, which was introduced by Bergstra et al. [4]. It was evaluated on
three different benchmark datasets using binary, gray, and depth data and two different

validation techniques. Moreover, the system recognized gestures from external operators
and LaRED datasets in real time. Ozcan and Basturk [30] proposed a gesture recognition
architecture based on the AlexNet model in the later work. The hyperparameters of the
architecture were optimized by a heuristic optimization algorithm based on artificial bee
colonies.
The Microsoft Kinect camera can provide three types of data: skeleton, depth, and color,
so the camera has been widely used to collect gesture data. Li and Tang et al. [21] proposed
to use the Microsoft Kinect camera to acquire depth and color images, and a joint bilateral
filtering algorithm was used to denoise the depth images. Then the depth information was
used to segment the hand area in the color image. A convolutional neural network was
used to complete the feature extraction part, and finally, a support vector machine (SVM)
was applied as a classifier. In the end, the network achieved 98.52% accuracy on eight
gestures.
Tao et al. [45] proposed a CNN to realize American Sign Language letter recognition
and used multi-view image augmentation and inference fusion. The multi-view augmenta-
tion strategy referred to using the Microsoft Kinect camera to capture a gesture depth image
and 3D point cloud. Then set additional virtual cameras to face the point cloud of differ-
ent perspectives so that a set of additional images were generated, and these images were
subjected to some preprocessing, and the unnecessary arm information was removed, and
finally, a depth image for training was obtained. In the inference step, the method included
information from multiple views for the final prediction to solve the confusion caused by the
orientation change and partial occlusion. On the two benchmark datasets, the most advanced
methods were achieved.
Nagi et al. [27] proposed a deep neural network that combines convolution and max-
pooling operations (MPCNN). Gesture images were collected by humans wearing colored
gloves and using mobile robots and then extracted to the hand area using color segmenta-
tion, performed morphological operations, and eliminated noise. The network classification
accuracy rate of the six gestures achieved 96%. And the robot using an ARM 11 533MHz
processor, realized real-time hand gesture recognition.
Mohammed et al. [26] proposed a deep learning-based architecture to jointly detect
gestures and classify gestures. The architecture used a one-stage dense object detector Reti-
naNet to extract the hand area and a lightweight convolutional neural network MobileNet
for gesture recognition. The method of the hand gesture detection part was tested on four
public datasets, including the Oxford, 5-signers, EgoHands, and Indian classical dance
(ICD)datasets, and the average precisions (AP)obtained were 72.10% and 97.90%, 93.10%,
and 85.50%, respectively. The method of hand gesture recognition part was evaluated on the
LaRED and the simple and complex TinyHands datasets, and the accuracy rates obtained
were 97.25%, 99.00%, and 90.43%.
All in all, the vast majority of the above works are based on the CNN architecture, and
the datasets used are relatively single. The ViT-based model proposed in this paper does not
require the CNN architecture at all. Simultaneously, multiple datasets including publicly
available self-made dataset are used to verify the availability of the model. For the self-made
dataset in this paper, the depth information of the Kinect V2 camera is used to complete the
foreground segmentation of the gesture image, and then the forearm region in the gesture
image is removed, thereby reducing unnecessary features in the process of gesture feature
extraction. At the same time, the above related works all use standard data augmentation
strategies to varying degrees. This paper explores the impact of different data augmentation
strategies on gesture image classification.

3 Proposed method
3.1 Foreground segmentation
As we know, the Kinect V2 camera released by Microsoft provides not only RGB images
of traditional cameras but also depth and human skeleton information, and the camera can
simultaneously capture these three kinds of information [44]. The depth information retains
the three-dimensional shape of the object and has invariance to the difference in lighting
and the change of the background. The Kinect V2 allows remote interaction between the
hand and the camera within a range of 0.5 m to 4.5 m.
At the same time, the Kinect V2 can map the depth information to the color image.
According to this, the camera can separate the hand region of interest from the back-
ground. The depth threshold is used to filter out the background. Through the corresponding
relationship between the depth information and the color information, a color image of
the foreground containing only the region of interest of the hand is obtained. The hand
region of interest is extracted as shown in Fig. 1. As shown in Fig. 1(b), after the above
method, only the image of the difference z (the value of z is 350 mm) between
the maximum threshold and the minimum threshold is retained, thereby eliminating the
human body and the background part, and obtaining the gesture image A containing the
arm part.
3.2 Arm removal
To reduce the calculation time when extracting features and classifying gestures, removing
unwanted forearm regions from the image is necessary. The arm removal method in this
paper is based on the distance transformation algorithm and eight-connected discrimination
algorithm [51]. The distance in the distance transformation algorithm refers to Euclidean
distance.

following steps:

radius, and then make the pixel values in the circle all 0 to get an image with the palm part
removed.
Then the maximum pixel value of the remaining area in the image D is detected and take
it as Pmax, which corresponds to the pixel point P. When there is an arm area in the image,
P is usually located at the midline of the arm (pixel point P in the Fig. 2), and Pmax is
relatively large; when there is no arm area in the image, P is generally located at the midline
of the finger, and Pmax is relatively small. Because the gesture data collected in this study
all include arms, the situation without arms is not considered.
Step 4: Use the eight-connected discrimination algorithm to find the label values of all
areas. Suppose the label value of the currently connected area is the same as the label value
of the pixel point P . The pixel value of this part is replaced with 0, and the resulting binary
image contains only fingers and the palm of the part where the fingers are connected.
Step 5: Perform an XOR operation between the results obtained in Step 3 and Step 4, the
binary image containing only the arm area is obtained.
Step 6: Perform an XOR operation between the result obtained in Step 5 and the binary
image H of the original hand to obtain the binary image of the gesture after removing the
arm.
Step 7: Perform a mask operation between the binary image obtained in Step 6 and the

3.3 Vision Transformer
The Vision Transformer is a computer vision model based entirely on the Transformer archi-
tecture [48] originally designed for text tasks. The Vision Transformer was proposed to
extend the standard Transformer to computer vision tasks. The Transformer architecture has
shown the best performance in machine translation and other NLP tasks in recent years, so
that it has attracted the interest of many people [12]. For image classification tasks, everyone
thinks of the Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), such as VGGNets [40]. The traditional
CNN architectures usually use filters with local receptive fields. The Vision Transformer
is a completely different architecture from the traditional CNN, and it has a more unified
representation in all layers. In addition, the method of the Vision Transformer for image
classification is to use the encoder module of the Transformer to map a series of image
patches to the semantic labels. After exploration [36], it is found that the difference between
the Vision Transformer and the CNN is that its self-attention mechanism plays a key role,
and its mechanism can enable the early aggregation of global information. And the multi-
head self-attention module in the Vision Transformer also contains residual connections,
which can learn better global information.
The overall system architecture is shown in Fig. 3. The system architecture can be com-
posed of a linear embedding layer, an encoder, and a final classifier. Before entering the
linear embedding layer, the image X of size c × h × w (where c represents the number of
channels of the image, h is the height of the image, and w is the image’s width) from the
training set needs to be subdivided into non-overlapping patches. Then each patch is treated
as a separate token in the Transformer. Each patch of size c × p × p is extracted from the
image, thus forming a series of patches (x1, x2, . . . , xn) of length n, where n = hw/p2.
Usually, the p × p is 16 × 16 or 32 × 32, where a smaller patch resolution will lead to a
longer sequence.

3.3.4 Vision Transformer variants
Different versions of the Vision Transformer are proposed in [14]: the “ViT-Base”, the “ViT-
Large”, the “ViT-Huge”. The differences between these three versions are the number of
layers of the encoder, the size of the hidden dimension, the size of the MLP classifier, and
the number of attention heads used by the MSA layer. Table 1 shows a summary of the
various versions.
Because of the success of Visual Transformers on image tasks, variants of ViT such as
DeiT [46], Swin Transformer [22], CaiT [47] etc. have appeared. Touvron et al. [46] pro-
posed a competitive convolution-free transformer called Data Efficient Image Transformer
(DeiT) by training only on the ImageNet database. It uses a CNN as a teacher model and
performs a knowledge distillation strategy on the Transformer to integrate the inductive bias
of the Transformer. Swin Transformer [22] enhances the Transformer’s local feature extrac-
tion capability by performing local attention within a window and introducing a method
of shifting window partitions for cross-window connections. CaiT [47] mainly includes the
self-attention stage and the class-attention stage. In the self-attention stage, there is no class
token, and in each layer, a learned diagonal matrix initialized by small values is exploited to
update the channel weights dynamically, thereby offering a degree of freedom for channel
adjustment. The class token is added in the class attention stage.

4.3 Experimental setup
In the first set of experiments, this paper used different data augmentation strategies to
evaluate the Vision Transformer’s performance in processing augmented data. In the second
set of experiments, this paper explored the effect of changing the image size on the model’s
overall accuracy.
In all experiments, the ViT-Base model (ViT-B/16) was used in this paper, and the set-
tings are as follows [14]. The model was composed of 12 encoder layers, and each layer had
12 attention heads. Its embedding dimension was 768. In this paper, a model pre-trained on
Imagenet-21k was used, and then fine-tuned on Imagenet-1k. To fine-tune on the gesture
datasets, this model used 50 epochs of training, and if there was no better score after wait-
ing for 5 epochs, an early stopping strategy was implemented. The batch-size was set to 16.
This paper used the Cross-Entropy loss function to learn the network’s weights. The Adam

5 Conclusion
This paper uses the depth threshold of the Kinect V2 camera to segment the foreground
part of interest. The depth information of the obtained image is mapped to the color space
to obtain the gesture image containing the arm. Next, the eight-connected discrimination
algorithm and the distance transformation algorithm remove the redundant arm part. Finally,
a gesture image dataset that does not contain the arm is obtained.
This paper proposes a static hand gesture recognition method based on the Vision Trans-
former. This model uses a self-attention mechanism to capture the long-term dependencies
between image patches. The evaluation was conducted on the self-made dataset and the
other two datasets, and certain data augmentation strategies were used to improve the clas-
sification accuracy of gesture images. The recognition accuracy obtained was 99.44%,
99.37% and 96.53%, respectively. At the same time, a larger image resolution was used for
evaluation, and the recognition accuracy obtained was 99.64%, 99.38% and 97.86%, respec-
tively. The accuracy is improved, but the difficulty of training increases and the training
time becomes longer. Compared with the method based on CNN, the recognition accuracy
of this model is higher.

Although this paper has demonstrated the usefulness of the ViT for hand gesture recog-
nition tasks, there are some limitations. First of all, there are few literatures on the use
of visual Transformers for hand gesture recognition tasks, so this paper only refers to the
application of the CNN models in hand gesture recognition tasks. Second, the ViT model
has more parameters than the most CNN models, which makes training more difficult and
time-consuming, and a small model is more beneficial for deploying gesture recognition.
Therefore, the future research direction is mainly to apply more visual Transformers to hand
gesture recognition tasks in order to obtain better generalization models. And reduce the
parameters of the ViT model, making it more favorable for deployment.
