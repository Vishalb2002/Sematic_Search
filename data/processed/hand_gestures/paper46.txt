Hand Gesture Signature Recognition
with Machine Learning Algorithms
Azra Izni Anis binti Azlin, Pang Ying Han, Khoh Wee How,
and Ooi Shih Yin
Abstract Hand gesture recognition is regarded as an imperative Human–Computer
Interaction technology for non-verbal communication and interaction between
human and computers. Inspired by hand gesture recognition systems, hand gesture
signature recognition is proposed to replace the existing online handwritten signa-
ture recognition which requires a stylus pen and tablet as middleware between users
and the systems. The possibility of germs contamination on these shared devices
alerts public concerns, especially during this COVID-19 pandemic. Hence, an in-air
signature recognition, termed as iSign, that utilizes hand gesture signing movement
is proposed in this paper. In iSign, a Kinect Sensor is adopted to detect and capture
the hand movement of the users while signing. This mode of data acquisition does
not require users for any direct touch on any devices. Next, the captured video
data is transformed into feature template. Various machine learning algorithms, i.e.
Bayesian network, Naïve Bayes, Decision Tree, Random Forest etc., are examined
to classify the hand gesture signatures. Empirical results demonstrate that Random
Forest performs the best among the machine learning algorithms with accuracy of
93%.

Introduction
Signatures have been counted as an identity authentication form in the society for
hundredsofyears.Signaturerecognitionisthemostusedapproachincommercialand
legal industries for personal authentication. There are two mode of signature analysis
methods: ofﬂine and online [1]. In ofﬂine signature mode, the signature patterns are
signed on papers and scanned. The digitized signature data is further processed and
analysed for veriﬁcation. Online signature system requires data acquisition devices,
such as stylus pen and tablet to capture the static and dynamic signing data such as
geometrical shapes, pressure sensitivity, velocity, acceleration, etc.
In these recent years, the world has been acutely affected by Coronavirus disease
(COVID-19). This global virus is an infectious disease and has a catastrophic effect
worldwide causing nearly 5.4 million deaths [2]. There are almost 280 million
conﬁrmed cases globally. This COVID-19 pandemic has accelerated the use of touch-
less technology due to hygiene desires. Hence, the classical ofﬂine and online hand
signature systems which involve shared or touch-based properties (i.e., paper, pen,
stylus pen, and tablet) should be replaced to minimize the spread of the virus through
the contaminated shared/common device. One of the alternatives is to use touchless
handwritten signature recognition technology.
Dynamic hand gesture is a pattern of a temporal sequence of points concerning the
hand centroid of the individual while performing a hand motion [3]. Hand gesture
recognition is a popular Human–Computer Interaction technology for non-verbal
touchless communication and interaction between humans and computers. Inspired
by the hand gesture system, hand gesture signature recognition is proposed to replace
the existing handwritten signature recognition as a means of touchless personal iden-
tity veriﬁcation technology. In this paper, an in-air handwritten signature recognition,
termed as iSign, that utilizes hand gesture signing movement is proposed. Signing
using hand gestures offers several discriminative characteristics for classiﬁcation
such as signature shape, hand motion orientation, position or trajectory, and motion
speed or direction. Orientation is how the hand is being rotated whether in left or
right directions; the signature shape is regarding how the signature is formed due to
the unique signing behaviour of individuals; position or trajectory is about how users
move their hands while signing; motion speed or direction is how fast the hands’
movements are when the users are signing.
The iSign system is non-intrusive and reliable since the hand gesture signature
is utterly untraceable. The touchless characteristic of iSign resolves the hygiene
issue and diminishes the likelihood of the outspread of contagious viruses. In iSign,
there are four main phases: signing data acquisition, data pre-processing, feature
extraction/projection and data classiﬁcation. The overview of iSign is illustrated in
Fig. 1. During the data acquisition, a Microsoft Kinect Sensor is adopted to detect and
capture the hand signing movement into a video format. This mode of data acquisition
does not require users for any direct touch on any devices. Next, the captured video
data is pre-processed for enhanced quality before data analysis. In this phase, pre-
processing processes such as region of interest localization, background subtraction,

Related Works
Since Microsoft Kinect sensor is the main acquisition device in iSign, a brief descrip-
tion of this sensor is discussed here. The affordable price and ample availability of
Kinect sensor propagate the application of Kinect sensor in the gesture recognition
[4]. In recent years, Kinect camera is gradually replacing time-of-ﬂight camera and
stereo camera in Human–Computer Interaction community for visual computation.
This is because Kinect camera is not only able to capture colour information, but
it also affords depth information as an additional knowledge for computer vision
processing [5]. Hand gesture signature recognition can be grouped as an online
signature recognition. The core difference between both is that the latter is using a
stylus pen and digital tablet for signature acquisition; whereas, the former is using
camera, i.e., Kinect sensor, to capture the signing movement of the hand. In literature,
Dynamic Time Warping is one of the popular techniques used in online signature
recognition [6, 7]. Tian et al. [8] proposed in-air password recognition using a Kinect
camera. Password signing trajectory was computed by associating the user’s ﬁngertip
in each video frame. Then, Dynamic Time Warping was employed to analyse and
classify the signing trajectory. There is another similar work that utilizes the depth
information captured by the Kinect sensor for hand gesture signature recognition [9].
In this work, the positional trajectory was determined by connecting the ﬁngertip and

Furthermore, a personal identiﬁcation for home entertainment based on hand
gesture signature was proposed [10]. In this work, point distribution model and neigh-
bourhood components analysis were utilized for measuring the signature trajectory
distance. Instead of using Kinect camera, Google glasses was adopted as a data acqui-

ﬁnger movement of the hand gesture. Motion vectors were computed by using Optical
Flow calculation. Besides, Gaussian Mixture Modelling and Principal Component
Analysis were used for binary mark representative feature. Lastly, Dynamic Time
Warping is used for signature classiﬁcation. Sun et al. [12] presented a hand gesture
recognition for personal veriﬁcation to unlock mobile phone. This system utilized
three-dimensional (3D) acceleration information. A template updating strategy was
proposed for persistent template update to tackle the physiological variations of the
hand gestures.
Besides, an imagery-based hand gesture signature recognition system was
proposed [13]. This system manipulated both temporal and spatial information of
the signing hand gesture. To be speciﬁc, hand movement orientation and hand shape
were examined to learn the hand gesture signature. This rich feature analysis resulted
an encouraging performance in personal identiﬁcation and veriﬁcation. Additionally,
the authors further improved their hand gesture signature recognition with multi-view
spatiotemporal analysis based on spectral histogramming [14]. In this system, data
feature learning is performed via ﬁlter convolution and nonlinear feature analysis
through a series of feature binarization, block-wise histogramming and histogram
concatenation for a global descriptor. In recent years, deep learning methods have
beenexploredinhandgesturerecognition.Forinstances,aLongShort-TermMemory
(LSTM) had been applied in a hand gesture recognition based on trajectories features
[15]. This proposed deep learning model was constructed for self-learning the spatial,
temporal, andDoppler features of thehandmotions. Yuet al. [16] proposedadynamic
hand gesture recognition based on 2D convolutional neural network and feature
fusion. The authors adopted a fractional-order model to extract optical ﬂow frames
of the data video and then incorporated the fractional-order into the neural network.
Empirical results showed a promising performance of this deep learning approach.

The Proposed System
As aforementioned, iSign system comprises of four phases: signing data acquisition,
data pre-processing, feature extraction/projection and data classiﬁcation. Microsoft
Kinect camera is employed to capture the hand gesture signature data. The captured
data is in a video format, a sequence of depth images which describes the hand motion
of a subject while signing. Next, this raw data is pre-processed to segment the region
of interest (ROI). The nearest point from the camera (i.e., the nearest ﬁngertip from

the camera) is utilized to trace the signing motion. There is always a challenge that the

be possible. This results the loss of signing motion tracing, as illustrated in Fig. 2.
A predictive palm segmentation method is proposed to assure the hand allocation
precision. More explanation of the method can be found in this paper [17].
After the pre-processing phase, the resultant data is a 3D binary spatio-temporal
imagery sequence, as illustrated in Fig. 3. From the ﬁgure, we can observe that the
resultant data embraces the detected hand location, hand shape, signing motion and
speed. Next, feature projection is performed to produce a compact but informative
feature template. In this work, x-proﬁle projection is computed by projecting the
3D binary imagery sequence along the y-axis. This projection unveils the pattern of
the time and signing motion horizontally in a compact projected template. Besides,
y-proﬁle projection is calculated by projecting the 3D sequence along the x-axis,
exhibiting the pattern of the time, and signing motion vertically. The x-proﬁle and
y-proﬁle projections are illustrated in Fig. 4.
After the feature projection phase, the projected templates, i.e., x-proﬁle and
y-proﬁle, are further analysed using multiple machine learning algorithms for signa-
ture classiﬁcation. In this work, the performances of the classiﬁcation algorithms,
such as Bayesian network, Naïve Bayes, Decision Tree, Random Forest and nearest-
neighbour classiﬁer, in recognizing hand gesture signatures are analysed using
WEKA software, a java-based machine learning tool.
Bayesian Network is a probabilistic graphical model, composing modes and
directed edges, captures conditionally dependent and independent relationships
between random variables. It adopts Bayesian inferences for probability computa-
tions. Naïve Bayes is an effective classiﬁer algorithm based on the class conditional
independence assumption. This classiﬁer estimates the likelihood of a feature vector
being connected with a label. J48 decision tree is a statistical classiﬁer that could

Database and Experimental Protocol
In this work, the proposed system is assessed by using a self-collected Hand
Gesture Signature (HGS) database [13]. By using a Microsoft Kinect camera, image
sequences of hand signing movement are recorded in a video format. There were 100
volunteers participated in this data collection with age ranging from 18 to 40 years.
Each participant contributed 20 HGS samples and these HGS samples would be sepa-
rated into training and testing sets depending on the implemented testing protocol.
Training set is used to train the system model; whereas testing samples are new and
unseen to the system model, i.e., there is no overlap in samples between the training
and testing sets and used for system performance evaluation.
Two testing protocols were carried out in this work: cross-validation protocol and
testing split protocol. In the ﬁrst protocol, tenfold cross-validation was performed.
The HGS database was partitioned into ten equal-sized subsets. A single subset was
held for testing the model, and the remaining nine subsets were integrated and used
for model learning. The cross-validation process was repeated for 10 folds with each
of the 10 subsets was used once as the testing sample set. Then, the obtained ten
results from the folds were averaged for performance indicator. In the testing split
protocol, the HGS database was randomly split into two subsets: testing and training
sets. In this work, different ratio of splitting was performed with 60:40, 70:30 and
80:20 (ratio of training set: testing set).

can be deduced that the integrated features (i.e., xy-proﬁle) is able to provide richer

best performance in this application. Random Forest improves on bagging since it
could decorrelate the individual tree with the splitting on a random subset of features.
In other words, at each tree split, Random Forest only considers for a small subset
of features, instead of the entire features of the model.

mental results, it is observed that the ratio of 80:20 training-test samples shows higher
accuracy rates. More training samples always supply extra information which helps
machines to learn the underlying data structure and make predictions. In other words,
the utilization of more training samples can produce better recognition. It is because
different kinds of data characteristics are available and extracted from multiple data
samples to provide richer information for feature analysis. More training samples,
more opportunities for the learning algorithms to learn and understand the underlying
mapping of the input data to the output.

Conclusion
Thispaperpresentsacontactlesshandgesturesignaturerecognitionbyusingmachine
learning technology. In this system, there are four stages to recognize the detected in-
air hand gesture handwritten signatures: data acquisition, data pre-processing, feature
transformation and data classiﬁcation. Data acquisition is to detect and capture the
signing hand movements using Microsoft Kinect. In data pre-processing, the quality
of the captured data is enhanced. Next, the pre-processed data is transformed to
the desired form. In this stage, directional projections are performed vertically and
horizontally to generate x-proﬁle and y-proﬁle. Lastly, classiﬁcation is performed.
In this work, multiple machine learning algorithms are explored, such as Random
Forest, Nearest-neighbour classiﬁer, Bayes Net, Naïve Bayes, and J48 Decision Tree.
Empirical results show that Random Forest classiﬁer is able to achieve the best
hand gesture signature recognition performance among the other machine learning
classiﬁers.
