ABSTRACT: The
 advancement
 of
 wearable
 sensing
technologies demands multifunctional materials that integrate
high sensitivity, environmental resilience, and intelligent signal
processing.  In  this  work,  a  flexible  hydrophobic  conductive
yarn  (FCB@SY)  featuring  a  controllable  microcrack  structure
is developed via a synergistic approach combining ultrasonic
swelling and non-solvent induced phase separation (NIPS). By
embedding  a  robust  conductive  network  and  engineering
microcrack  morphology,  the  resulting  sensor  achieves  an
ultrahigh  gauge  factor  (GF  ≈ 12,670),  an  ultrabroad  working
range  (0%–547%),  a  low  detection  limit  (0.5%),  rapid
response/recovery  time  (140  ms/140  ms),  and  outstanding
durability  over  10,000  cycles.  Furthermore,  the  hydrophobic
surface  endowed  by  conductive  coatings  imparts  exceptional
chemical stability against acidic and alkaline environments, as
well as reliable waterproof performance. This enables consistent functionality under harsh conditions, including underwater
operation. Integrated with machine learning algorithms, the FCB@SY-based intelligent sensing system demonstrates dual-
mode  capabilities  in  human  motion  tracking  and  gesture  recognition,  offering  significant  potential  for  applications  in
wearable electronics, human–machine interfaces, and soft robotics.

 1    Introduction
The  rapid  advancement  of  information  technology  and  the
increasing  demand  for  personalized  health  management  have
positioned wearable devices as a pivotal component of the Internet
of  Things  (IoT)  era  [1−4].  These  devices  are  designed  for
portability,  comfort,  and  seamless  integration  into  daily  life,
enabling individuals to proactively manage their health. At the heart
of wearable device functionality lies sensor technology, which plays
a  crucial  role  in  achieving  comprehensive  and  accurate
physiological  monitoring  [5−9].  To  ensure  a  practical,  long-term,
and  comfortable  user  experience,  wearable  sensors  must  be
lightweight,  soft,  flexible,  and  even  stretchable,  allowing  them  to
adapt  to  the  intricate  and  dynamic  contours  of  the  human  body
[10−13]. However, traditional strain sensors often rely on rigid or
semi-flexible substrates, compromising flexibility and user comfort,
especially  under  conditions  involving  repetitive  or  extensive
deformation [14−17].
Recent  research  has  extensively  explored  yarn-based  strain
sensors for their ease of weaving, flexibility, and comfort [18−22].
However,  achieving  a  balance  of  excellent  stretchability,  high
sensitivity,  and  stability  remains  challenging  [23−25].  Leveraging
the significant mismatch in Young’s modulus between conductive
coating and flexible matrix, tension-induced crack structures have
gained attention as a strategy to enhance sensor sensitivity [26−30].
For  instance,  Guo  et  al.  [31]  engineered  multilevel  structured
sensing yarns with cracks embedded in wrinkle patterns using wet-
spinning  and  mechanically  guided  assembly,  achieving  a  wide
sensing  range  (0%–150%)  and  ultrahigh  sensitivity  with  gauge
factors (GF) spanning from 174 to 298,000. Similarly, Wang et al.

[32] designed hierarchical microcracked strain sensors leveraging a
heterogeneous  surface  strategy  integrating  silver  nanowires
(AgNWs)  and  MXene,  where  the  tension  stress-induced
microcracks  result  in  outstanding  sensitivity  (GF  ≈ 244),  a  wide
linear sensing range (≈ 60%), and rapid response times (< 30 ms).
Notably, the precise formation of microcrack architecture remains a
significant challenge in the development of an expansive sensitive
sensing  domain.  Unlike  conventional  crack-generation  strategies
relying on uncontrolled deformation, the pre-stretching technique
enables deterministic tuning of crack density, depth, and uniformity
by  adjusting  the  pre-applied  strain  level.  This  controllable
microcrack engineering allows for a tradeoff between sensitivity and
sensing  range,  which  is  difficult  to  achieve  in  uncontrolled
systems [33, 34].
Furthermore,
 the
 rapid
 advancement
 of
 immersive
communication and artificial intelligence has driven the integration
of machine learning with flexible yarn-based strain sensors, creating
an  advanced  platform  for  interactive  intelligent  systems  that
seamlessly  connect  the  physical  and  digital  realms  [35, 36].  By
leveraging  high-quality  sensor  data,  machine  learning  algorithms
can  identify  patterns,  classify  human  movements,  and  predict
actions  based  on  learned  motor  behaviors.  This  synergy
significantly  broadens  the  applications  of  yarn  strain  sensors,
ranging  from  real-time  activity  monitoring  to  complex  gesture
recognition systems for human–robot interaction and soft robotics
control.  Moreover,  integrating  machine  learning  enhances  the
accuracy  and  robustness  of  activity  recognition  while  enabling
adaptive  and  personalized  monitoring  systems  [37].  By  training
models on extensive human movement datasets, these systems can
detect  subtle  variations  in  gestures  or  movements,  making  them
ideal  for  applications  such  as  rehabilitation,  sports  performance
analysis,  virtual  reality,  and  assistive  technologies  for  individuals
with mobility impairments [38].
In  this  study,  we  present  a  flexible  hydrophobic  yarn-based
conductive  composites  (FCB@SY)  with  a  controllable  microcrack
structure for yarn-based strain sensor, which is further enhanced by
a  machine  learning  approach  for  precise  gesture  recognition.  As
illustrated  in Fig.  1,  the  obtained  FCB@SY  possesses  excellent
ultrasensitive  strain  sensing  capacity  to  achieve  real-time
monitoring of diverse human body movements (such as finger and
knee bending) and physiological signals (including pulse and vocal
cord  vibrations).  A  machine  learning  framework  integrated  with
the sensor allows accurate and efficient gesture classification, with
promising  applications  in  health  monitoring  and  sign  language
recognition.  This  approach  not  only  advances  wearable  sensing
technology but also opens new possibilities for intelligent systems in
healthcare,
 human–machine
 interfaces,
 and
 soft
 robotics.
Additionally,
 the
 hydrophobic
 coating
 provides
 excellent
waterproofness  and  strong  resistance  to  both  acidic  and  alkaline
corrosion,  ensuring  reliable  strain-sensing  performance  across
diverse  environments.  This  durability  makes  it  well-suited  for
everyday  wear,  including  challenging  conditions  such  as
underwater applications.

 2.2    Fabrication of hydrophobic conductive yarn
The spandex yarn (5 cm) was first cleaned by sonication in a 1:1
ethanol/deionized  water  mixture  for  10  min.  After  washing,  the
yarn  was  respectively  pre-stretched  to  strains  of  0%,  50%,  100%,
and 150% and secured with clamps on each end. The pre-stretched
yarn was then immersed in the diluted CB slurry (20 mL CB paste,
20 mL deionized water, 40 mL DMF) and sonicated for 30 min,
followed by rinsing in deionized water for an additional 30 min to
remove excess CB from the yarn’s surface. During this process, the
swollen spandex matrix undergoes phase separation, allowing some
CB particles to embed within the yarn matrix. The samples were
then dried in an oven at 60 °C for 2 h. After drying, the clamps
were  removed  to  gradually  release  the  pre-strain,  forming  the
CB@SY  conductive  yarn.  These  yarns  were  then  immersed  in  a
0.02 wt.% PFDT/ethanol solution for 50 min, rinsed with deionized
water, and dried at 60 °C for 2 h to yield the FCB@SY hydrophobic
conductive  yarn.  Here,  the  FCB@SY  samples  prepared  under
different pre-strain levels are referred to as FCB@SY-x, where “x”
indicates the pre-strain percentage applied.
 2.3    Characterizations
The structure morphology and element composition of the samples
were examined using a scanning electron microscope (SEM, JEOL
JSM-7500F,
 Japan)
 equipped
 with
 an
 energy
 dispersive
spectrometer  (EDS).  Raman  spectroscopy  (DXR,  GX-PT-2412,
Thermo, Waltham, MA, USA) with a 633 nm laser was employed
to  analyze  the  molecular  structure  of  the  samples.  Water  contact
angle  (WCA)  and  sliding  angle  (SA)  measurements  were
conducted  using  an  optical  contact  angle  device  (SL200KS),  with
the reported values representing the average of five measurements
from different locations.
Both ends of the hydrophobic conductive yarn (FCB@SY) were
evenly  coated  with  conductive  silver  paste,  and  copper-nickel
conductive  tape  was  attached  as  electrodes  to  fabricate  assemble
strain  sensors.  A  test  setup  comprising  a  digital  multimeter
(DMM4050)  and  a  universal  testing  machine  (UTM2203,  Sun
Technology  Co.,  Ltd.,  Shenzhen,  China)  was  utilized  to  evaluate
strain response performance. Current–voltage (I–V) characteristics
at  varying  strains  were  measured  using  an  electrochemical
workstation with a voltage scan range of −5 to 5 V at a scanning
rate of 0.05 V/s. The fabricated strain sensors were also affixed to
different  body  parts  using  transparent  and  breathable  medical
polyurethane (PU) tape to measure human physiological and multi-
channel electrical signals corresponding to various gestures. During
underwater sensor testing, the electrodes at both ends of the sensor
were sealed and secured using waterproof medical-grade PU tape,
effectively preventing potential issues such as electrode corrosion or
signal attenuation. A portable Bluetooth-enabled wireless resistance
measurement device (TruEbox 01RC, LinkZill) was employed for
signal acquisition and transmission. The written consent from all
participants have been obtained before the sensing experiment.
 3    Results and discussion
Figure 2(a) illustrates the comprehensive fabrication procedure of
the  high  stretchable  FCB@SY  hydrophobic  conductive  yarn,
involving  straightforward  solvent-assisted  swelling,  nonsolvent-
induced  phase  separation  (NIPS)  and  surface  hydrophobic
treatment
 techniques.
 The
 fabrication
 commences
 with
ultrasonication-assisted  immersion  of  pre-stretched  spandex  yarn
(p-SY)  in  a  CB/DMF/H2O  ternary  mixture.  During  this  stage,
ultrasonic
 energy
 generates
 high-intensity
 micro-jets
 and
shockwaves  that  drive  rapid  CB  particle  migration,  facilitating
repeated interfacial collisions with the yarn surface to ensure strong
CB  adhesion.  Simultaneously,  DMF  in  the  mixed  solvent  also
induces polymer chain relaxation through solvent swelling, creating
expanded  interstitial  spaces  within  the  spandex  matrix  for
subsequent  CB  entrapment.  The  subsequent  NIPS  process  is
triggered  by  transferring  the  swollen  composite  (CB@p-SY)  into
aqueous  medium,  where  the  rapid  solvent/non-solvent  exchange
leads  to  polymer  chain  rearrangement  that  partially  encapsulates
CB  nanoparticles  within  the  contracting  spandex  matrix,  thereby
forming  an  interpenetrated  CB  conductive  layer  with  enhanced
interfacial stability for the resulting CB@p-SY. Here, the successful
construction  of  conductive  layer  can  also  create  a  modulus
difference  with  the  pristine  yarn  matrix,  which  can  induce
microwrinkle formation on CB@SY surface upon releasing the pre-
stretching  that  generates  asymmetric  recovery  stresses  at  the
CB/matrix interface. Finally, the microstructured surface of CB@SY
is  further  treated  with  low  surface  energy  PFDT  to  converts  the
conductive  CB@SY  into  the  superhydrophobic  FCB@SY  while
preserving  its  electrical  percolation  network,  completing  the
multifunctional yarn fabrication.
Figure 2(b) shows the macroscopic morphologies of original SY
and  FCB@SY  prepared  under  varying  pre-strain  conditions.
Obviously,  the  pristine  white  SY  undergoes  complete  chromatic
transition  to  black  after  surface  functionalization,  confirming  the
homogeneous deposition of CB nanoparticles and the formation of
a continuous conductive coating. Moreover, all FCB@SY specimens
exhibit irreversible residual deformation compared with the pristine
SY,  and  the  magnitude  of  permanent  strain  shows  strong
dependence  on  the  applied  pre-stretch  amplitude.  Notably,  the
sample  prepared  at  the  maximum  pre-strain  of  150%  retains
approximately 40% of its initial length after removing the constrain,
demonstrating  pronounced  strain  locking  behavior.  This  can  be
explained by the fact that a higher pre-strain level is beneficial for
more CB to be encapsulated in the yarn surface during the solvent-
assisted  swelling  and  NIPS  process,  causing  a  higher  surface
modulus that generates stronger asymmetric recovery stresses at the
CB/matrix
 interface,
 ultimately
 stabilizing
 the
 elongated
morphology  through  energy  dissipation  at  the  interface.  The
mechanical  compliance  of  the  functionalized  yarn  is  further
demonstrated through its programmable deformability and shape
resilience. As illustrated in Fig. 2(c), the FCB@SY can wrap around
cylindrical  substrates  with  a  diameter  of  4  and  10  mm  without
structural  failure,  and  it  can  also  be  repeatedly  weave  into  a
bowknot with complete shape recovery after unraveling. All these
confirms  that  the  intrinsic  flexibility  and  elastic  recovery  of  the
spandex matrix remain uncompromised despite the integration of
rigid CB conductive domains.
Raman spectroscopic analysis was further conducted to provide
critical insights into the structural evolution of the composite yarns
(Fig.  2(d)).  Clearly,  the  original  spandex  yarn  exhibits  the  typical
symmetric/asymmetric  C–H  stretching  peaks  at  2916  and
2862 cm−1 and the backbone C=C stretching at 1613 cm−1, which are
well consistent with its polyurethane-ether chemical architecture. In
contrast,  the  CB@SY  spectrum  reveals  two  prominent  carbon-
specific  bands  at  1350  cm−1 (D-band)  and  1590  cm−1 (G-band),
confirming successful integration of CB [39]. Among them, the D
peak indicates the defects in the C atom lattice, while the G peak

The  strain  sensing  properties  of  various  FCB@SY  samples  are
systematically  examined.  As  depicted  in Fig.  4(a),  the  relative
resistance change (ΔR/R0, ΔR = R − R0, R0 is the original resistance,
R is  the  resistance  under  applied  strain)  progressively  increases
across the entire strain range for all samples. It is noteworthy that
the  ΔR/R0 value  of  the  FCB@SY  sensors  under  the  same  applied
strain decreases with increasing pre-strain, while their sensing range
expands  accordingly.  This  is  further  demonstrated  in  their
corresponding  strain  sensitivity  (gauge  factor,  GF  =  (ΔR/R0)/ε,
where ε denotes the applied strain) analysis curves (Fig. 4(b) and
Fig.  S2  in  the  ESM).  In  particular,  FCB@SY-0%  has  the  highest
sensitivity  at  100%  strain,  but  its  sensing  range  is  only  273%,
whereas FCB@SY-150% has a strain sensing range of up to 653%,
but the sensitivity is only 16 (at 100%). This suggests that moderate
pre-stretching  can  effectively  increase  the  crack  density  and
optimize the reconfiguration efficiency of the conductive network,
and  that  adjustable  strain  sensing  behavior  can  be  achieved  by
precisely  controlling  the  processing  technique  to  meet  different
application  requirements.  This  adjustable  strain  sensing  behavior
stems  from  the  layered  dual-network  structure  of  FCB@SY  yarn,
which comprises a surface microcrack conductive layer and a CB
network  embedded  within  the  matrix.  After  pre-stretching  is
released,  the  modulus  mismatch  between  the  CB  layer  and  the
matrix  generates  microcracks.  Higher  pre-strain  produces  denser
cracks, enabling dynamic regulation of conductive pathways during
stretching to achieve high sensitivity. Meanwhile, the CB network
embedded within the matrix remains unaffected by surface cracks,
ensuring  conductive  continuity  under  large  deformations  and

can still maintain a good connection, which is crucial for ensuring
the effectiveness of the sensor over a wide strain range, even if the
surface  conductive  layer  experiences  extreme  strain-induced
destruction. Consequently, the synergistic interaction between the
upper ultrasensitive microcrack structure and the underlying stable
conductive  network  confers  upon  the  FCB@SY  strain  sensor  an
extensive response range and exceptional sensitivity. Meanwhile, it
is  evident  that  the  tunable  strain  sensing  behavior  of  FCB@SY  is
derived  from  its  deliberately  designed  pre-strain  level,  which
impacts  the  structure  of  the  CB  conductive  layer  on  the  yarn
surface,  as  depicted  in Fig.  3.  Consequently,  it  produces  varying
synergistic  interaction  effects,  thereby  facilitating  distinct  strain
sensing mechanisms and diverse strain sensing performances.

 4    Conclusions
In summary, we present a scalable preparation strategy for high-
performance  flexible  strain  sensors  based  on  hydrophobic
microcracked  conductive  spandex  yarn  (FCB@SY).  Through  the
synergistic  effect  of  solvent  swelling,  strong  ultrasound-assisted
dispersion,  and  NIPS  technology,  the  deep  embedding  of  the
conductive  network  in  the  yarn  matrix  and  the  formation  of  a
controllable
 microcrack
 morphology
 are
 achieved,
 which
significantly  improves  the  electrical  response  performance  and
mechanical stability of the sensor. Specifically, the FCB@SY sensor
exhibits  ultra-high  strain  sensitivity  (GF  ≈ 12,670),  ultra-wide
operating  range  (0%–547%),  low  detection  limit  (0.5%),  fast
response/recovery time (140 ms/140 ms), and excellent durability
under more than 10,000 cyclic loadings. The PFDT-treated coating
has excellent hydrophobicity and chemical stability, allowing it to
operate reliably in acidic, alkaline, and underwater environments.
In  addition,  the  sensor  can  effectively  detect  vigorous  human
movements and subtle physiological signals, and when combined
with  machine  learning  algorithms,  the  FCB@SY-based  intelligent
sensing system can further improve the accuracy and efficiency of
gesture
 recognition,
 offering
 the
 potential
 for
 advanced
human–computer  interaction  applications.  This  work  provides  a
versatile  and  scalable  platform  for  the  development  of  next-
generation  fiber-based  flexible  electronic  devices  with  promising
applications  in  health  monitoring  and  adaptive  sensing  under
extreme environmental conditions.
