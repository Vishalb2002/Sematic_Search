Automated Recognition of Hand Gestures From Multichannel EMG Sensor
Data Using Time–Frequency Domain Deep Learning for IoT Applications
Amlan Deep Mohapatra1
, Amol Aggarwal2
, and Rajesh Kumar Tripathy1∗
1Department of Electrical and Electronics Engineering, BITS-Pilani, Hyderabad Campus, Hyderabad 500078, India
2Department of Pharmacy, BITS-Pilani, Pilani Campus, Pilani 333031, India

Manuscript received 15 April 2024; accepted 10 May 2024. Date of publication 13 May 2024; date of current version 21 May 2024.
Abstract—The automated recognition of hand gestures from multicha-
nnel electromyogram (MEMG) sensor data is essential for human–
computer interaction, prosthetic control, rehabilitation, and biometric-
related applications. This letter proposes a time–frequency domain
deep neural network (TFDDNN)-based approach to recognize hand
gestures using MEMG recordings. The MEMG recordings are seg-
mented into frames, and the average of all channel information
(mean EMG signal) for each frame is evaluated. The continuous wavelet transform is applied to the mean
EMG signal to obtain the joint time–frequency representation (TFR). The TFR-based image of the mean EMG
signal is used as the input to a deep representation learning network (DRLN) model to recognize hand ges-
tures. Two publicly available databases are utilized to evaluate the performance of the proposed TFDDNN ap-
proach. The results show that the proposed TFDDNN has obtained overall accuracy values of 92.73% and
80.33% using MEMG signals from database 1 and database 2 for multiclass-based recognition of hand ges-
tures. The suggested DRLN model has demonstrated higher accuracy for automated hand gesture recognition
than existing deep-learning-based methods. We have deployed the proposed TFDDNN approach in a web ap-
plication for Internet of Things (IoT)-enabled real-time recognition of hand gestures using MEMG sensor data.
Index Terms—Sensor signal processing, accuracy, continuous wavelet transform (CWT), deep representation learning, electromyogram
(EMG), hand gestures, Internet of Things (IoT).
I. INTRODUCTION
The electromyogram (EMG) signal deciphers the muscle’s electrical
activity during contraction, and it is used for neuro-muscular disease
diagnosis [1] and human–machine interface (HMI) related applica-
tions [2], [3]. The automated hand gesture recognition system using
EMGsensordataishelpfulinapplications,suchasassistivetechnology
for elderly and disabled people, prosthetic limb control, virtual reality,
biometrics, gaming, and smart home devices [4], [5]. It consists of
recording multichannel EMG (MEMG) sensor data, extraction and
selection of features from the MEMG data, and recognition of ges-
tures using machine learning techniques [3]. The deep learning-based
methods have the merits that these are as fully automatic as those of
the manual feature-based machine learning techniques for automated
gesture recognition using MEMG signals [6]. The Internet of Things
(IoT)-enabled hand gesture recognition enables the real-time monitor-
ing of EMG sensor data for HMI applications [7]. Hence, developing a
new deep learning-based approach and deploying this approach on the
cloud-based framework are attractive for recognizing hand gestures
from MEMG data.
In recent years, various deep-learning methods have been developed
to recognize hand gestures using MEMG signals. The deep convolu-
tional neural network (CNN) model has been used for hand gesture
classiﬁcation using surface MEMG signals [8]. The authors in [8]

II. MEMG SIGNAL DATABASES
In this work, we have used two publicly available databases to
assess the performance of the presented TFR-based DRLN approach
to recognize hand gestures. Database 1 (Mandely database) contains
four-channel EMG signals (sampling frequency as 2000 Hz) from
40 subjects for ten classes (one rest class and nine hand gesture
classes) of hand gestures [15]. The MEMG signals have been recorded
using a four-channel BIOPAC MP36 device with the channel locations
as forearm muscles for ten different types of simulating gestures
performed by each subject [15]. Similarly, database 2 (Ninapro db4)
contains the MEMG signals (sampling frequency as 2000 Hz) from
ten subjects for 53 classes (1 rest gesture and 52 hand movement
gestures) of hand gestures [16]. The gesture activities, such as ﬁnger
movements, grasping movements, functional movements, and basic
wrist movements, are utilized while recording MEMG signals in
database 2 [16].
III. PROPOSED METHOD
The ﬂowchart illustrating the proposed TFDNN-based approach is

A. Frame-Based Segmentation
In this work, frame-based segmentation is utilized to divide the
MEMG recordings of each subject into frames for different gesture
classes. For database 1, the 6-s nonoverlapping window segments
four-channel EMG recordings into MEMG signal frames. The anno-
tations for the starting and ending samples of the MEMG recordings
for each hand gesture class are given in the database [15]. Based on
these points, we have evaluated the MEMG signal frames from all
40 subjects for each gesture class. For each gesture type, ﬁve MEMG
signal frames are considered after the segmentation from database 1.
Thus, for all 40 subjects and ten gesture classes from database 1, 2000
MEMG signal frames are evaluated. Similarly, for database 2, a sliding
window of length 260 ms (520 samples) and an overlapping of 235 ms
(470 samples) are utilized to segment the MEMG recording of each
gesture class [6]. After segmentation, we selected only ten MEMG
signal frames for each gesture class for all subjects. Therefore, for all

C. Deep Representation Learning Network
The architecture of the DRLN model is depicted in Fig. 3. The input
to this model is the TFR-based image with size as 128 × 128 × 3.
The DRLN model uses a frozen XceptionNet-based transfer learning
block [18] to obtain the representation or feature map from the TFR-
based image of the mean MEMG signal frame. The size of the tensor
obtained after the frozen XceptionNet is given as 4 × 4 × 2048 [18].
AfterthefrozenXceptionNetblock,theﬂattenedlayerobtainsafeature
vector of size 1 × 32786. The dropout layer, dense layer, and softmax
layer (output) are used after the ﬂattened layer in the suggested DRLN
model for the automated recognition of hand gestures. The number of
trainable and nontrainable parameters for the DRLN model are 327800
and 20861480, respectively. For the database 1 case, the dense layer
after the dropout layer in the DRLN model architecture contains ten
neurons. However, for the database 2 case, as we have 53 classes, the
number of neurons in the dense layer is 512. The hyperparameters
used during the training of the DRLN model are the number of epochs
as 13, batch size as 32, optimizer as “Adam,” and loss function as
“sparse categorical cross-entropy,” respectively [13]. The number of
neuronsinthedenselayerandthehyperparametersoftheDRLNmodel
are selected using the grid-search-based approach. In this study, the
training and test MEMG signal frames are selected based on both
subject-dependent (hold-out validation and ﬁvefold cross-validation
(CV)[19]andsubject-independentvalidation(SIV)techniques[6].For
SIV cases, 70% and 30% of the MEMG signal frames from different
subjects are used to train and test the proposed DRLN model for
recognizing hand gestures. The MEMG frames from subject numbers
3, 18, 21, 24, 31, and 36 of database 1 and subject numbers 2, 5, and 8
from database 2 are used in the testing phase of the DRLN model for
the SIV case.

CWT-based TFR images of mean MEMG signals with “Morlet”
mother wavelet as compared with “Mexican-hat” and “Shannon”

CWT-based analysis of MEMG signals. We have quantized the DRLN

with FP16 and INT8 quantization cases using MEMG signals from
database 1 are 87.74% and 82.32%, respectively. Hence, we have
deployed the ﬂoating point 32-bit version of the proposed TFDDNN
approach on a WAPP for IoT-enabled real-time recognition of hand
gestures. The hand gesture prediction for an MEMG signal on WAPP is
shown in Fig. 5. The advantages of the proposed work include real-time
monitoring, accessibility, and scalability. Similarly, the high latency,
privacy, and security of MEMG data from the subjects and dependency
on internet connectivity are the limitations of the proposed method for
IoT-enabled hand gesture recognition.
V. CONCLUSION
A time–frequency domain deep learning-based approach has been
proposed in this work to recognize hand gestures using MEMG signals.
The TFR-based image has been evaluated using channel averaging of
the MEMG signal and CWT. The DRLN model has been employed to
recognize hand gestures automatically using TFR images of MEMG
signals. The proposed approach has produced accuracy values of
95.71% and 86.56% for the 10-classes and 53-classes-based SDV for
handgesturerecognitionusingMEMGsignals.FortheSIV-basedhand
gesture recognition task, the suggested approach has demonstrated
accuracy values of 92.73% and 80.33% for 10-classes and 53-classes-
based recognition of hand gestures.
