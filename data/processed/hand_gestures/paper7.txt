ABSTRACT The use of collaborative robots (aka cobots) - a type of robot designed to work alongside
humans have recently increased. To create a seamless collaboration between human and cobot, hand
gesture is the most widely used. In this paper, we focus on addressing the hand gesture recognition for
human-cobot interaction. Recently, many models have been proposed for hand gesture recognition using
various modalities, including RGB, depth, and skeleton data. However, the majority focus on recognizing
isolated hand gestures on a segmented video sequence which limits their practical application. In this paper,
we propose two methods for continuous hand gesture recognition, namely RGB-CoGes and SkeRGB-
CoGes. The RGB-CoGes method relies solely on RGB image sequences, while SkeRGB-CoGes integrates
both skeleton and RGB modalities. Both methods share a common training phase, in which an isolated
gesture recognition model based on VideoMAE and VideoMAEv2 is trained on segmented sequences.
During inference phase, RGB-CoGes employs a sliding window approach on continuous sequences to
classify gestures within each window using the trained model. In contrast, SkeRGB-CoGes leverages
the lightweight skeleton information to detect the start and end points of gestures, after which the same
trained model is applied to the identified segments. Additionally, to evaluate and promote the research on
continuous hand gesture recognition, a self-built dataset named CoboGesture containing 19 different hand
gestures in the context of human-cobot interaction has been collected and fully annotated. Experiment results
demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework in enhancing gesture recognition performance
for human-cobot interaction. Regarding continuous recognition, the proposed methods RGB-CoGes and
SkeRGB-CoGes achieved frame-wise accuracies of 0.87 and 0.90, and overlap scores of 0.68 and 0.67,
respectively, significantly outperforming the skeleton-based methods. Furthermore, we demonstrated the
successful deployment of the proposed method on an edge device.

In traditional methods, humans usually used additional
hardware devices, such as hand-held controllers or computers
with keyboard and mouse, to interact with robots. These
methods make operators feel uncomfortable because they
can not directly work with robots while performing their
tasks. Moreover, extra devices and software come with
additional costs, and the fact that robots cannot adapt well

to small-scale factories. These drawbacks resulted in the
development of promising and cost-effective techniques
without any additional device, called vision-based technol-

benefits that enhance the usability, efficiency, and safety
of robotic systems in various applications. This approach
allows workers to freely interact with cobots without any
equipment and involves the ability of robots to respond to
human hand gestures. This task, also known as computer
vision for robots, is a crucial process that enables robots
to identify and respond to the complex human behavior.
Visual information is captured through a camera system
mounted on robots, then used to help them understand the
activities required by humans and make informed decisions
on how to interact or assist the person. In the literature,
many studies have aimed to achieve both high accuracy
and real-time response in human-cobot interaction tasks [3],
[4], [5], [6], [7], [8], [9], [10], [11]. Despite these efforts,
several challenges persist when these systems are deployed
in real-world environments. These challenges include the
diversity of human activities, background clutter, varying
lighting conditions, different camera viewpoints, and partial
occlusion. To capture visual data for hand gesture recognition
(HGR), various types of cameras are employed, such as
RGB cameras, thermal cameras, and infrared cameras, each
offering unique advantages and limitations based on the
application scenario.
It is worth noting that existing HGR methods are gen-
erally divided into two categories: isolated recognition and
continuous recognition. The isolated recognition approach
focuses on dealing with video segments containing only a
single hand gesture and treats the task as a video classification
problem. The objective is to detect and classify a specific
gesture within a pre-defined video segment. In contrast,
continuous recognition involves processing multiple gestures
in real-time and generating results that correspond to a
sequence of gesture labels over time. This task is significantly
more challenging than isolated recognition, as it requires
not only classifying but also determining the precise timing
and duration of each gesture within a continuous video
stream. Accurate temporal localization of gestures is critical
for continuous recognition, making it a more complex
problem.
This paper introduces a deep-learning framework designed
to handle continuous hand gesture for human-cobot interac-
tion task. The proposed framework comprises two crucial
stages that are isolated recognition and post-processing.
While isolated recognition operates as a video classifier,
identifying individual gestures within segmented sequences.
Post-processing aims at refining the isolated results to ensure
smooth and accurate outputs for continuous gesture recogni-
tion, thereby enhancing the system’s overall performance in
real-time applications. For isolated recognition, transformer-
based VideoMAE [12] is exploited and its decoder is replaced
by a linear convolutional layer to predict the class for each
interested video input. These isolated results are fed forward
to post-processing to provide the final results for continuous
recognition.
Additionally, the lack of training data leads to a low
performance for any computer vision problem. In fact, there
are a few publicized hand gesture datasets for continuous
recognition task. This is the motivation for us in this
work to build a novel continuous hand-gesture dataset for
human-cobot interaction. Inspired by the gesture design of
Barattini et al. [13], our self-built dataset named CoboGesture
consists of total 150 untrimmed videos of 50 subjects with
19 hand-gestures. To resemble an industrial environment,
dataset is collected in a laboratory with many computers
and electronic devices in the background, and flashing illu-
mination. Cluttered background and bad lighting conditions
can pose some challenges to the dataset, requiring the HGR
method to be robust with the environment.

II. RELATED WORKS
Some early studies on HGR problem have relied on data
collected from hand-held devices integrated into wristbands
or hand gloves [14], [15]. However, this type of data
suffers from noise and inconvenience, making this approach
unappealing in industrial environments. Consequently, recent
research has focused more on vision-based approaches, using
cameras as the data source. This approach is not only more
effective and explainable but also allows for the inheritance of
previously studied architectures and techniques in computer
vision. In this section, several remarkable studies related to
vision-based HGR are briefly discussed. Additionally, several
existing datasets for HGR task will be also analyzed.
A. HAND GESTURE RECOGNITION
1) ISOLATED HAND GESTURE RECOGNITION
To recognize the isolated gestures, some early studies
leverage the ability of 3D-CNN models for building spatio-
temporal representation. In the work of Li et al. [16], a spatio-
temporal attention-based ResC3D model was introduced to
learn a compact and effective representation for hand gestures
by leveraging both spatial and temporal domains. This model
combines the strengths of a residual network with a C3D
model to enhance recognition performance. Similarly, in [17],
the authors proposed a multi-modal approach where a uni-
modal 3D CNN was used for feature extraction, supported
by multi-modal training knowledge. Additionally, Spatio-
temporal Semantic Alignment (SSA) loss was applied to
optimize the model’s performance.
Although 3D-CNN models have achieved certain results,
they are really complex and inefficient due to capturing both
spatial and temporal information. Based on this observation,
Yu et al. [18] proposed a 2D-CNN modal to fuse multi-
modal data, RGB for spatial and and optical flow for temporal
information. In [5], a Multi-scale Parallel Convolutional
Neural Network (MPN) with high-resolution and low-
resolution channels was introduced, using RGB, depth, and
EMG signals to enhance recognition. In [19], the author
introduced a 2D-CNN and LSTM model combination to
learn short-term and long-term video features, with RGB and
optical flow fused for 2D-CNN input, followed by LSTM for
sequence learning. 3D-CNN and ConvLSTM networks have
been also proved to be effective for dynamic hand gesture
recognition [4], [20].

Existing methods using RGB images often face compu-
tation complexity issues, leading to inefficiency. To address
this, some work focuses on extracting skeleton data to reduce
complexity while maintaining recognition rates [21], [22],
[23]. In [24], the authors proposed a CNN-based model
estimates 3D hand keypoints, which are then projected into
three views for better hand gesture representation. This
framework uses 3D-CNNs to learn spatial and temporal infor-
mation, improving recognition rates. In [22], a combination
of OpenPose and ST-GCN was used for isolated hand gesture
recognition, with OpenPose capturing skeleton information
and ST-GCN classifying gestures. Inspired by ST-GCN, [21]
developed a new hand skeleton model using three types of
edges to represent joint actions. Despite significant progress,
this approach only handles segmented videos, limiting its
real-life application. Recently, research has shifted towards
continuous hand gesture recognition.
2) CONTINUOUS HAND GESTURE RECOGNITION
Compared to isolated hand gesture recognition, the contin-
uous approach is considered more realistic and challenging.
Continuous hand gesture recognition methods must not only
classify the activity but also accurately determine its start
and end times. These methods typically follow a two-
step framework. In the first step, isolated hand gesture
recognition is applied within a sliding window to assign
an activity label to the observed segment. Then, results are
refined by a post-processing step [5], [25], [26], [27], [28].
In the literature, the sliding window is the most widely
used techniques for video segmentation. Isolated recognition
methods are applied to each video segment to predict the
corresponding hand gesture. In [26], the authors introduce
a general pipeline for continuous hand gesture recognition.
A sliding window is used to split an input video into several
segments. Each of these segments will be analyzed to predict
whether it corresponds to a hand gesture. This approach
allows the model to process continuous video streams and
identify gestures as they occur within the segmented frames.
Additionally, a prediction will be filtered if its confidence
score is smaller than a given threshold. This helps to ensure
safety in human-cobot interaction. To enhance the accuracy
of continuous hand gesture recognition, a majority voting
mechanism is proposed in the work of [27]. This involves
saving predictions from a fixed time window into a voting
box. For each step, the final prediction is determined by the
class with the highest number of votes within that window.
This method helps in smoothing out the predictions and
reduces the impact of noise, leading to more reliable and
consistent gesture recognition results. In [28], the authors
have made a great effort to build an effective framework
based on skeleton information to address continuous hand
gesture recognition. This framework includes two crucial
phases: hand detection and hand recognition. With the use of
TD-Net (Triple Feature Double Motion) [29], a lightweight
and robust recognition model, the obtained recognition rates

B. CURRENT DATASETS FOR HGR
Hand gesture recognition is a rapidly growing field in
recent times. Current hand gesture recognition models have
achieved high accuracy and can be applied in various settings
such as entertainment, medical, and industrial applications.
However, this field still faces significant challenges including
the complexity of gestures, variations among individuals, and
diverse background environments.
The availability of hand gesture datasets was limited
until 2007 and has only increased recently. To date, many
datasets have been publicized, providing a large number
of frames across multiple modalities (RGB, Depth, Optical
Flow, Skeleton, etc..). However, most gestures in these
datasets are originally defined for interacting with screens
(e.g., swipe, tap) or are related to daily life activities (e.g.,
sweeping the house, pouring milk) rather than for interaction
with robots. In this section, some existing datasets for hand
gesture recognition are briefly mentioned.
IPN-Hand [30] is a large-scale benchmark dataset col-
lected from 50 different subjects, consisting over 800,000
frames and 4,000 gesture instances. All these frames are
recorded at a frame rate of 30 FPS with a resolution
of 640 × 480 pixels. In this dataset, each participant
performs continuously 21 gestures with three random breaks.
Among them, 13 gestures are specifically designed for
interaction with touchless screen in both static and dynamic
scenarios. To date, IPN-Hand is considered as one of the
most challenging datasets for gesture recognition because
of strong variations in background and lighting conditions.
Additionally, even though originally built for touchless screen
manipulation, the defined set of gestures can still be mapped
to a new set of commands for HCI tasks, despite the potential
loss of semantics.
InHARD [31] is an industrial dataset for Human Action
Recognition, created to facilitate Human-Robot Collabo-
ration in industrial environments. This dataset involves
16 subjects performing in the assembly task with the
help of a UR10 Cobotic arm, each subject conducted the
task two to four times. It includes video and skeleton
modalities collected from three different points of view.
InHARD dataset encompasses 13 main actions combined
with 16 common objects in the industrial workplace, resulting
in a total of 72 high-level classes that describe the actions
more accurately. The InHARD dataset primarily focuses on
recognizing workers’ actions in an industrial environment,
rather than capturing the interaction between the worker and
the collaborative robot (cobot). Additionally, to accurately
capture the human skeleton, workers are required to wear
motion sensors, which can occlude parts of their hands.
As a result, RGB-based methods may face challenges in this
context
SHREC [32] is a 3D dynamic hand gesture dataset that
provides sequences of hand skeletal data combined with
depth images. This dataset contains 14 dynamic hand gestures
collected with Intel RealSense camera from 28 subjects. Each
gesture is performed from 1 and 10 times by each subjects in
two scenarios: using one finger and using the whole hand,
resulting in 2,800 image sequences with a resolution of
640 × 480. However, this dataset support only the isolated
gesture evaluation.
Jester [33] was introduced in 2019 and, at that time, was
the largest vision-based hand gesture dataset. It consists of
148,092 short clips, each 3 seconds long, resulting in more
than 5 million frames. A total of 1,376 volunteers were
involved in data collection, under significant variations in the
background. Similar to the SHREC dataset, the Jester dataset
is dedicated to evaluating isolated gesture recognition.
NVGesture [34] is designed for touchless driver control
applications and recorded with three modalities: RGB, Depth,
and Infrared. It includes a total of 1,532 dynamic hand
gestures of 25 gesture classes collected from 20 subjects.
This dataset focuses on gesture recognition in automo-
tive environments, using RGB-D and infrared to collect
data.
HANDS [35] is an RGB-D dataset designed for HCI
purposes with 15 static gestures. Depth frames are recorded
using a Kinect v2 sensor and later aligned with RGB frames.
Despite the dataset’s various backgrounds, lighting condi-
tions, and changing subject positions, it has limitations. The
dataset is recorded with only 5 subjects, each corresponding
to 2400 RGB-D frames, resulting in a total of 400 seconds of
clips. Additionally, videos recorded from subjects are filtered
to discard blurred and occluded frames and segmented to
150 frames per gesture, which inadvertently disables training
and benchmark on continuous HGR.
VIVA challenge’s dataset [36], [37] is a multi-modal
dynamic hand gesture dataset designed for studying natural
human activities in real-world driving environment. This
dataset involves 885 intensity and depth video sequences col-
lected by a depth camera Microsoft Kinect. These sequences
performed by 8 subjects inside a vehicle and classified into
19 different dynamic hand gesture classes. A key feature
of the VIVA dataset is its challenging conditions, including
cluttered backgrounds, fluctuating illumination, and frequent
occlusion, making it ideal for evaluating the robustness of
gesture recognition methods.
EgoGesture [38] is a multi-modal large-scale dataset built
for egocentric hand gesture recognition. This dataset has a
total of 2,081 RGB-D videos of 50 distinct objects recorded
from six complex indoor and outdoor scenes with strong
variations in illumination and background. These videos
result in 24,161 gesture samples divided into 83 classes of
both static and dynamic gestures, and are used for both
isolated and continuous hand gesture recognition.

III. CoboGesture - A NEW HGR DATASET FOR
HUMAN-COBOT INTERACTION
A. MAIN REQUIREMENTS AND GESTURE SET DESIGN FOR
HUMAN-COBOT INTERACTION
In the industrial environment, production lines are dynamic,
with equipment and working positions frequently updated
to accommodate different projects. This environment is
often noisy, with machinery sounds and alarm signals
making verbal control of robots challenging. While solutions
like attached microphones or wearable sensors, such as
inertial measurement units embedded in wristbands or hand
gloves, cause inconvenience to the users, especially in the
environment having a narrow working space. Furthermore,
the scenario in which one operator takes over from another
must be considered. In such cases, the new operator will often
not have the necessary tools and equipment immediately
at hand. Exchanging devices between operators can be
time-consuming and may also lead to issues such as size
mismatches. It is evident that the use of additional devices
presents significant drawbacks in the field of human-cobot
interaction.
Thanks to the successes in vision-based approaches in
recent years, an image dataset would be preferred over
wearable sensors, which are uncomfortable and less intuitive.
Compared to vocal commands that can only be recognized
from a short distance, gestural commands are understandable
at a wider range, as long as the camera provides an image
of good enough quality. Considering these factors, a vision-
based dataset for hand gesture recognition in industrial
settings must be developed. This dataset would help address
the challenges of gesture complexity, variations among
individuals, and diverse background environments, ensuring
effective human-robot collaboration.
Based on the above analysis, in this paper, we aim at
building a continuous hand gesture dataset for human-cobot
interaction, namely CoboGesture dataset. With the objective
of fostering collaboration between workers and robots, the
dataset must be ensure to meet the following criteria:
• One-Handed Gestures: Workers are expected to inter-
act with robots while holding tools. Therefore, gestures
should be performed with one hand, allowing for either
right or left hand usage to accommodate different
preferences.
• Versatility Across Various Robots: The dataset should
be applicable to a broad range of robotic applications.
Consequently, the proposed gesture set must be compre-
hensive enough to cover the use cases for these two types
of robots.
• Ease of Learning: The gesture set should be compact
and straightforward to learn, minimizing the training
time and effort required for new operators. The gestures
can be either static or dynamic but must be intuitive and
contextually appropriate. For example, pointing fingers
to indicate direction or nodding to signify agreement.
By adhering to these requirements, the dataset will be prac-
tical and effective for enhancing human-robot collaboration
in industrial environments. This approach ensures that the
dataset addresses the unique challenges of the industrial
workspace while being intuitive and easy to implement for
operators.
In [13], a set of 19 hand gestures have been carefully
designed by taking into account all aforementioned require-
ments. Therefore, in our study, we collect a video dataset of
these 19 gestures. Additionally, an extra class ‘No-gesture’ is

IV. PROPOSED METHOD FOR CONTINUOUS HAND
GESTURE RECOGNITION
In this paper, we propose two methods for continuous
hand gesture recognition methods named RGB-CoGes and
SkeRGB-CoGes. RGB-CoGes relies only on RGB image
sequences whereas SkeRGB-CoGes leverages both skeleton
and RGB images. Figure 5 illustrates RGB-CoGes and
SkeRGB-CoGes methods. The two methods share the
training phase where an isolated recognition model is
trained on segmented sequences. In the inference phase,
RGB-CoGes applies the sliding window technique on the
continuous sequences and determine the gesture class within
the windows based on the trained model. Finally, several
post-processing techniques are employed to refine the results
and generate the final continuous gesture recognition output.
In SkeRGB-CoGes, to leverage the lightweight of skeleton,
we employ skeleton information to order to detect the starting
and ending point of a gesture. Then, the trained model is
also applied in the determined sequence. Finally, some voting
techniques are applied to refine the recognition results.
A. ISOLATED GESTURE RECOGNITION
VideoMAE model [12] is a deep learning model designed
for self-supervised learning on video data. It is inspired
by the success of MAE (Masked Autoencoders) [39] used
for image-based tasks but extend this concept to video
processing. The main objective of VideoMAE is to efficiently
learn spatio-temporal representations from unlabeled video
data by masking a large portion of input frames and training
the model to reconstruct the missing content. This process
of masking and reconstructing forces the model to gain
a deeper understanding of the structure and semantics of
the video, enhancing its ability to generalize and perform
various downstream tasks like classification, detection,
or segmentation. For this proposed framework, VideoMAE
model is treated as a video classifier for isolated recognition.
The structure of VideoMAE model proposed in [12]
consists of two crucial component that are encoder and
decoder. In comparison with MAE model, VideoMAE has
to address with two more challenging issues: (1) temporal
redundancy and (2) temporal correlation. It is clear that there
is no significant difference between consecutive frames in
a given input video and result in redundant information.
To overcome this challenge, a temporal down-sampling
block is adopted to reduce the number of input frame
as well as enhance the efficient video pre-training. This
helps save computational resources while still effectively
capturing meaningful information from the frame sequence.
As described in Figure 5, given an input video includes τ
frames, then T frames are chosen through sampling process
with a pre-defined temporal stride parameter, referred as
stride (in this work, stride = 4), resulting a set of images I ∈
RC×T×H×W . After that, these sampled frames are divided
into some non-overlapping patches and masked with high
ratio at 95% before forwarding to the encoder of VideoMAE
model. Noted that, for VideoMAE tube masking is applied
over frames in the input sequence. The remaining blocks are
then flattened via a 3D convolutional layer with a stride size
of (2 × 16 × 16), where each block has the same size as the
stride and is referred to as a token embedding. Additionally,
each token is combined with its positional information before
being passed to the encoder stage.
For the encoder block, ViT-L/16 [40] is utilized as
backbone to learn the representation embeddings from the
unmasked input data. ViT-L/16 is a ‘‘large’’ version of
Vision Transformer [40], pre-trained on the Kinetics-700
dataset [41]. It comprises 24 layers and operates with a
patch size of 16 × 16, converting input data into patch
embeddings that capture the video’s conceptual information.
To handle the temporal correlations between consecutive
frames, a space-time attention block is integrated into the
model. This block allows the model to learn both spatial and
temporal information simultaneously, which results in more
effective cube embeddings for the input video. Moreover, the
Space-Time Attention block plays a key role in understanding
the dynamic nature of videos by leveraging information
across both dimensions. It’s important to note that while
Multi-head Self Attention (MHSA) within the model can
be computationally expensive due to the complexity of
the attention mechanism, using a masking ratio of 95%
significantly reduces the computation burden. By masking
a large portion of the input data, the model focuses on

learning from limited but crucial visual cues, optimizing both
efficiency and effectiveness in the learning process.
The target of the decoder in the VideoMAE model is to
reconstruct the original video from the encoded unmasked
patches. Typically, this decoder functions as a smaller trans-
former that processes both masked and unmasked patches,
allowing the model to learn meaningful representations by
predicting the masked parts of the video. However, in our
framework, we modify this model by replacing the decoder
block with a linear convolutional layer specifically designed
for classification tasks. For this, the linear convolutional layer
takes the encoded features and generates output data that are
passed through a softmax function. The softmax function
converts these outputs into probability distributions across
the different classes. The class with the highest probability is
then selected as the predicted result, making this modification
tailored for classification rather than video reconstruction.
This approach simplifies the architecture while focusing
on effective video classification. Subsequently, the authors
in [42] proposed VideoMAEv2, which incorporates a dual
masking strategy. Similar to VideoMAE, its encoder also
operates on a subset of video tokens under the encoder
mask. However, VideoMAEv2 differs in the design of its
decoder. Instead of processing all tokens, it employs a
decoder mask that restricts reconstruction to a selected subset
of visible tokens. Masking of the decoder reduces the input
length and is shown to further decrease computational cost
and improve memory efficiency. With these advantages,
we also deploy this dual masking strategy of VideoMAEv2,
pretrain it with the ViT-L/16 backbone on the Kinetics-
700 dataset for a fair comparison. After the self-supervised
phase, the model is used as a video classifier and is trained
for isolated recognition, following the training procedure
described above.
B. RGB-CoGes: A RGB BASED CONTINUOUS GESTURE
RECOGNITION METHOD
In general, continuous recognition builds upon the results of
isolated recognition. Consequently, a continuous recognition
framework comprises two crucial steps: isolated recognition
and post-processing. This combination of isolated recogni-
tion and post-processing forms the core of continuous gesture
recognition, effectively handling multiple gestures in real-
time scenarios. As aforementioned, for isolated recognition,
VideoMAE model is used as a classifier that processes
segmented videos, treating each gesture independently. In our
framework, a sliding window mechanism is exploited to pick
up a sequence of frames from the input video. These video
segments are considered as the input for isolated recognition.
After that, several post-processing strategies aim to refine
isolated results for continuous approach. These strategies will
be mentioned in more details as follows.
1) SAMPLING
A sliding window continuously slides over the input video
stream with a constant step, extracting segments of τ
consecutive frames at each step. The size of τ is a
crucial parameter, as it determines the temporal span of the
gestures being processed. The step parameter is defined with
consideration that each action spans multiple frames, but its
start and end times may not align exactly with the window
boundaries. If the stride is too large, an action may fall
between two windows and be missed. Therefore, the step

is chosen such that the overlap between two consecutive
windows is at least 50%. After that, each video segment is
sampled to form a sequence of T frames (T < τ) in the same
way of temporal sampling strategy for isolated recognition
task. Noted that, padding can be added into the end of video
through multiplying the last frame.
2) POST-PROCESSING
The post-processing step is essential for refining the results
and providing the final continuous recognition output. In this
framework, post-processing step involves three main tech-
niques that are smoothing, linking and removing, shown in
Figure 6. Given an untrimmed video, proposed model creates
a sequence of action probability scores for each frame. Firstly,
smoothing strategy aims at filtering out false prediction based
on the temporal coherence across consecutive frames. The
prediction probability of the current frame is the average
probability of it, the previous frame, and the following frame.
It excepts for the final frame which the result of probability is
equal to the highest prediction probability of frame. Because
we add padding by multiplying the final frame into the end of
video. Secondly, several discrete segments should be linked
to build a complete hand gesture. For this task, a suitable
threshold is defined to connect these video segments, called
the average time tavg. This means the condition to link two
video segments is to have the same label and the gap between
the end of the first segment and the start of the second one
must not exceed tavg. The value of tavg is selected based on
the validation set and represents the average motion duration
across all classes in the dataset.
C. SkeRGB-CoGes: A CONTINUOUS GESTURE
RECOGNITION COMBINING SKELETON AND RGB
SEQUENCES
Taking into account the computation complexity of using
only RGB images in real-time scenario, we combine the
use of skeleton data with the trained VideoMAE model to
form the SkeRGB-CoGes framework as depicted in Figure 5.
This framework is designed for online deployment, where
users perform actions directly and the model should return
results instantly within a time constraint. Our idea is to use
skeleton data first to determine the action boundary, or the
start and end points of one gesture in a continuous video
stream. The VideoMAE model will be called within that
boundary, leveraging RGB data for prediction. After that,
a simple voting mechanism is used to select the final label for
that action segment. The details of the implementation will be
described below.
1) POSE ESTIMATION
In this step, we utilize Mediapipe for the extraction of
keypoint coordinates from RGB frames. For each frame, the
skeleton data is represented as a tensor with shape (J × D),
which J is the number of keypoints and D denotes the
dimensions. For the CoboGesture dataset, we extract 3D
keypoints, which include 42 hand joints (21 per hand) and
6 upper-body joints for both left and right wrists, elbows and
shoulders. After concatenating, the result tensor for one frame
has the shape of (48 × 3).
2) ACTION BOUNDARY DETECTION

tation module is used to detect the boundary of a gesture by
identifying significant changes in hand movement over time.

3) PREDICTION AND VOTING
After the start point is detected, the trained VideoMAE
model is called to process RGB frames, until the end point
is identified. The model will process τ frames within the
sliding window with a constant step, and those τ frames are
also sampled in the same way of temporal sampling as in
isolated task before passing to the model. To provide the final
recognition output, a simple voting mechanism is deployed
instead of post-processing, where the label with the highest
frequency of appearance within a detected segment is selected
as its final label.
Combining action boundary detection module with this
simple voting is expected to enable real-time response of
the framework. Moreover, the VideoMAE model is called
only within a detected action segment, which could reduce
the computational cost for insignificant gesture parts. The
feasibility of this framework will be evaluated on the test set
and deployment on the edge device, and will be discussed
thoroughly in the next section.
V. EXPERIMENTS AND RESULTS
Different experiments are conducted to evaluate the per-
formance of the proposed method at both isolated and
continuous gesture recognition steps on our new CoboGes-
ture dataset. We also show a comparison of experimental
results with the IPN-Hand dataset [30] and EgoGesture
dataset [38]. Additionally, as there are numerous methods
have been proposed for skeleton-based human activity
recognition, we also perform experiments to compare the
RGB-based human gesture recognition with skeleton-based
human gesture recognition on our CoboGesture dataset.
To achieve this purpose, four methods that are DD-Net [44],
TDDNet (Text-Enhanced DD-Net) [45], ST-GCN [46] and
CTR-GCN [47] are used. DD-Net model introduced in [44]
for isolated recognition has been extended for continuous
action recognition [48]. TDDNet is an improved version
of DD-Net by integrating a text encoder based on CLIP.
ST-GCN is a well-known model capable of learning both
spatial and temporal patterns by representing the human
skeleton as a dynamic graph. CTR-GCN, on the other hand,
learns channel-wise topologies in a refinement manner which
demonstrate superiority over other state-of-the-art methods
for skeleton-based action recognition. An off the shelf method
for human pose estimation that is Mediapipe is applied to
estimate the skeleton sequence from RGB sequence.
With our CoboGesture dataset, we utilize NVIDIA Geforce
RTX 3090 GPU to train all the models and NVIDIA GeForce
GTX 1080 Ti for comparing the model performance on
IPN-Hand dataset [30]. All experiments are implemented
in Pytorch library [49]. The number of epochs is set to
25, with 5 warm-up epochs and an initial learning rate of
1.25 × 10−4. For all three datasets IPN-Hand, EgoGesture,
and CoboGesture, the values of tavg, τ, step, T are set to 2,
64, 30, 16 respectively.

VI. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORKS
In this study, we presented a continuous gesture recognition
dataset as well as a method for recognizing these hand
gestures in human-collaborative robot interaction. The pro-
posed method integrates VideoMAE, sliding window, and
post-processing techniques. Due to the lack of available
datasets for continuous gesture recognition in the context
of human-collaborative interaction, we collected and fully
annotated a new dataset named CoboGesture. This dataset
will be made available to the research community to promote
future research in human-collaborative interaction. Extensive
experiments were conducted to assess the performance of the
proposed method in both isolated and continuous recognition
tasks. The results confirm the robustness of the proposed
method, achieving 95.4% of accuracy for isolated recog-
nition, 87% of Frame-wise Accuracy, and 0.68 of activity
overlap score for continuous recognition in CoboGesture.
On the IPN-Hand dataset, the proposed method achieves an
accuracy of 78.81% for isolated gesture recognition. For the
continuous gesture recognition task, it also outperforms the
DDNet-based approach, achieving a frame-wise accuracy of
80% and a score of 0.37. Despite these promising results, the
proposed method still demands considerable computational
resources. Therefore, model optimization techniques such as
model quantization and model pruning should be investigated
to enable real-time performance, especially given the limited
computing capabilities typically found in robotic systems.
Second, end-to-end recognition models could be applied
to simultaneously detect the start and end timestamps
and classify gestures. Finally, since human communication
involves multiple modalities, human-robot interaction should
integrate other modalities, such as speech, in addition to
gestures.
