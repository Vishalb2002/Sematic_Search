Abstract:
Regarding increasingly complex scenarios in hand gesture recognition (HGR), it is challenging to implement a reliable HGR due to the nonadaptability of individual
sensors to the environment and the discrepancy of personal habits. Multisensor fusion has been deemed an effective way to overcome the limitations of a single
sensor. However, there is a lack of research on HGR to effectively establish bridges linking multimodal heterogeneous information. To address this issue, we
propose a novel multimodal dynamic HGR method based on a two-branch fusion deformable network with Gram matching. First, a time-synchronized method is
designed to preprocess the multimodal data. Second, a two-branch network is proposed to implement gesture classification based on radar–vision fusion. The
input convolution is replaced by the deformable convolution to improve the generalization of gesture motion modeling. The long short-term memory (LSTM) unit is
used to extract the temporal features of dynamic hand gestures. Third, Gram matching is presented as a loss function to mine high-dimensional heterogeneous
information and maintain the integrity of radar–vision fusion. The experimental results indicate that the proposed method effectively improves the adaptability of the
classifier to complex environments and exhibits satisfactory robustness to multiple subjects. Furthermore, ablation analysis shows that deformable convolution and
Gram loss not only provide reliable gesture recognition but also enhance the generalization ability of the proposed methods in different field-of-view scenarios.

SECTION I.
Introduction
With the rapid development of communication technologies [1] and the Internet of Things (IoT) [2], many sensing devices have been deployed to
establish omnidirectional 3-D human–computer interaction (HCI). Hand gesture recognition (HGR), which is a noncontact HCI method with a high
frequency of use and a strong expressive ability, has become a popular research topic. HGR has been beneficial in facilitating human daily life and has
important applications in the interactive control of relevant scenes, such as vehicle-assisted driving [3], intelligent homes [4], sign language
interaction [5], intelligent robot control [6], virtual reality [7], and medical surgery assistance [8]. Moreover, noncontact measurement-based HGR is
very significant for avoiding the spread of global public health emergencies such as monkeypox and COVID-19 [9].

Gesture measurements can be divided into two main categories: contact devices and noncontact devices. Contact devices require equipment worn on
the body to collect hand motion data, such as surface electromyography (SEMG) signals [10], acceleration, and motion direction [11]. However, the
equipment is inflexible to wear and exposes users to the risk of bacteria or viruses. In contrast, noncontact devices, such as Wi-Fi-based, vision-based,
and radar-based sensors, can efficiently capture a variety of gestures and prevent physical interaction with the machine surface. However, due to the
limitations of Wi-Fi parameters [12] as well as electromagnetic interference and multipath reflection in the process of propagation, it is difficult to
distinguish fine-grained gestures through Wi-Fi-based methods.
As a common device for gesture acquisition, vision-based sensors are roughly composed of RGB cameras and depth cameras [13], [14]. For instance,
vision data gathered by the Kinect device are used to develop a natural gesture user interface for real-time tracking and gesture recognition [15], which
relies on directed search of the entire hand outline to recognize gestures. Compared with the high computational complexity of the depth camera, the
color camera can capture hand imaging at a lower power consumption [16]. In addition, due to the advantages of high frequency, short wavelength, and
broad bandwidth [17], millimeter-wave (MMW) radar has satisfactory spatial resolution to distinguish gestures [18]. Google’s Soli project [19]
produces a frequency-modulated continuous wave (FMCW) radar sensor to detect gestures. In recent years, radar-driven gesture recognition has been
used in vehicle entertainment systems and smart homes [20].
In recent decades, several algorithms have been used to model HGR tasks, such as Bayesian networks and HMMs [21]. These methods are only suitable
for simple gesture classification. Regarding complex HGR tasks, high-dimensional features are roughly extracted by convolutional networks (CNNs).
For instance, RGB images and depth images have been jointly inputted into a gesture classification model composed of a 3-D-CNN and a convolutional
long and short-term memory (LSTM) network to achieve isolated gesture recognition [22]. A lightweight multichannel CNN has been presented to
learn and classify radar-based gesture features, which are composed of radial distance, Doppler, and tangential azimuth–elevation [23]. In addition,
the Gram matrix is proven to be an effective method that extracts structural information [24]. The Gram matrix matched by dense interleaving is also
used to fit the feature gap between clean and degraded images [25]. Recently, Gram matrix matching has been used to maintain the complementary
structure of remote sensing data [26].
However, regarding increasingly complex scenarios of HGR, such as light-limited nighttime, bright outdoor, car interiors, smart homes with multiradar
working together, and indoor with high background noise, single vision-based or radar-based sensors do not reliably work in all these scenarios. On one
hand, visual sensors can provide good imaging information at low costs with sufficient optical information [27], but vision-based gesture acquisition
systems have also been greatly affected by lighting intensities [28]. On the other hand, energy scattering of gestures is captured by radar sensors to
extract the radial motion information [29]. Regarding scenarios with high background noise, the distribution of gestures in radar data will be drowned
out because gesture is a kind of micromotion with weak spectrum energy [30]. Therefore, complex scenarios will make a single sensor incapable of
meeting the needs of reliable gesture acquisition.
HGR has also been confused by the variability and ambiguity of gesture measurements in different distance field of view (FOV) scenes. For cameras,
near big and far small will lead to significant differences in the imaging size and details of the hand. For FMCW radar, different angles will lead to
changes in the measurement direction and range of the radial distance or Doppler. The energy of the radar signal will attenuate with an increasing
distance. Some studies have been proposed to address this issue. For instance, the RDAE domain is transformed to a 3-D Cartesian coordinate to
improve the spatial consistency of multilocation datasets [31], and deformable convolution has been embedded in the separable 3-D-CNN (S3-D) to
solve the spatiotemporal deformation caused by individual habits [32]. However, these solutions do not overcome the limitations of a single sensor.
Therefore, we propose a novel multimodal dynamic HGR method based on a two-branch fusion deformable network with Gram matching, which
provides a reliable solution for increasingly complex HCI environments. First, a time-synchronized method is designed to preprocess the time and
structure of multimodal data. Then, we propose a two-branch deformable HGR network with LSTM to address the gesture distortion in visual and radar
data and further extract spatial–temporal features. Finally, Gram matrix matching is presented as a loss function to mine the heterogeneous
information and fully exploit the complementary advantages of multimodal data. The main contributions of this article are as follows.

1) A novel dynamic HGR method based on radar–vision fusion is proposed to achieve deep linking of multimodal gesture information to cope with
increasingly complex HGR environments. We achieve reliable gesture recognition in six kinds of environments consisting of variable lighting and
different radar background noise.
2) Gram matrix matching driven by radar and vision data is first introduced into HGR classification to link high-dimensional heterogeneous
information between multimodal data. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work that calculates the Gram matrix to maintain the integrity
of information fusion of multimodal HGR.
3) A two-branch deformable network with LSTM is proposed to enhance the weak generalization of HGR with different users and FOVs. This work
completely takes advantage of the complementary heterogeneous information of different sensors while maintaining the temporal information of
multimodal data.
The remainder of this article is organized as follows. Related works are introduced in Section II. The fundamental and problem analyses are introduced
in Section III. The methodology is presented in Section IV. The experimental setup and experimental discussion are shown in Section V and Section
VI, respectively. Finally, in Section VII, concluding remarks are provided.
SECTION II.
Related Work
In this section, related works on vision-based HGR, radar-based HGR, and multimodal-based HGR are introduced.
A. Vision-Based HGR
The vision-based HGR can separate the hand from the environment through imaging information such as skin color, hand shape, and hand size [33].
Early work adopted dynamic time warping (DTW) for gesture recognition [34]. Then, hidden Markov models (HMMs), which mainly rely on the
extraction of sign language direction, trajectory information, and synthetic shapes, were introduced for HGR [35]. However, using HMM alone has
limitations, especially in training context-sensitive models [36]. In addition, some studies aim to estimate the state of hand joints to model 3-D
gestures, such as fitting joint angles by hand contours [37] and extremity movement observation (XMOB) upper body trackers [38]. Recently, Google’s
media-tube project [39] calibrates the hand region at the acquisition end to help gesture modeling. Through the machine learning (ML) method of
pretraining 30000 real and original images [16], the coordinates of 21 pixels of the hand in the image can be inferred to track the changes in the hand
state in the real-time system.
With the emergence of inexpensive consumer depth cameras, gesture recognition systems using depth cameras have also been introduced [40]. For
instance, depth camera data are inputted into the lightweight convolution neural network (CNN) model to effectively detect and classify video-based
gesture recognition [41]. However, vision-based methods must address well-known environmental challenges, such as brightness, contrast, and
exposure [42]. Vision-based gesture acquisition systems have been greatly affected by illumination intensity. Regarding indoor or light-limited
scenarios, reliable HGR is difficult to implement with a single visual sensor in a dark environment.
B. Radar-Based HGR

Recently, many radar-based methods have been studied to implement HGR through the range–Doppler–azimuth–elevation (RDAE) cube [43]. HGR
based on a complementary multifeature fusion network (CMFF-HGR) [44] has been designed to extract RDA features of a range–time map (RTM),
Doppler–time map (DTM), and angle–time map (ATM). These maps are obtained by fast Fourier transform (FFT) and multiple signal classification
(MUSIC) algorithms [45]. However, this work only achieves classification in simple gestures. To cope with the classification of more gestures,
spatiotemporal deformable and context-aware modules have been embedded into the classification model to mine the interrelation among time-varying
5-D feature spaces [32]. This work provides a standardized radar signal preprocessing procedure to encode multichannel feature information into a
time-varying 5-D feature spaces. By introducing CumSoft blocks on top of recurrent neural networks (RNN), a gesture triggering strategy is presented
to determine the appropriate time for early gesture prediction [46]. This work uses range–Doppler map (RDM) video to achieve early HGR. The
recognition rate can reach 88.85%, while the average time savings are 69.47%. The time–frequency map and spectrogram videos are inputted into a
dual-stream fusion deformable residual network embedded with a convolutional block attention module (CBAM) to implement robust HGR, which
achieves 97.5% classification accuracy [47].
However, regarding environments with high background noise, radar-based methods may not be reliably implemented. For instance, two vertically [48]
and three relatively [49], [50] placed radars have been used for capturing complementary writing data, concurrently operated radars have been
deployed to detect kitchen activities [51], and multiple radar systems have been designed for multiple indoor tasks and vital sign monitoring [52],
[53]. Multiradar working at the same time will produce significant mutual interference [54], which will increase the electromagnetic clutter energy and
decrease the performance of radar perceptions [55]. Gesture, which is a kind of micromotion, has relatively weak spectrum energy. Therefore, the
energy distribution of gestures will be obscured by high environmental background noise.
C. Multimodal-Based HGR
Compared with using only vision or radar sensors, multimodal fusion is considered to be an effective way to overcome the limitations of a single sensor.
For instance, gesture data captured by an ultrawideband (UWB) Doppler radar and wearable pressure sensors are inputted into an SVM classifier to
achieve simple human microgesture recognition [56]. Data gloves composed of pressure and flexible sensors are designed to capture gestures, while
these hand movements are classified by residual modules and LSTM [57]. These solutions inevitably face the limitations of wearable devices, while the
combination of noncontact methods will not suffer from such problems. The multimodal cross-learning method uses radar RDM, thermal imaging
camera images, and RGB camera images to train the automatic encoder model and only uses radar data for inference to achieve low-cost stove gesture
control [58].
Some methods study the radar–vision fusion of autopilot in the roughly complex and changeable environment of roads. For instance, a method based
on data-layer fusion is proposed: this method combines the weight and correlation consistency function of multisensor target recognition to construct
the data fusion support computing model [59]. Because radar and vision sensors have different physical properties and regions of interest, data-layer
fusion is difficult to directly implement. Another fusion method is decision-level fusion. Different networks are used to extract features from RGB
images and radar spectrum maps for common target recognition [60].
However, in contrast to the recognition and segmentation of large rigid objects (such as cars, trees, and roads) in the autopilot scene, the general objects
distinguished in the HGR scene are small nonrigid microgestures. Therefore, research on autopilot cannot be introduced for HCI. Time-of-flight (ToF)
data have been proposed to be combined with radar data to achieve human tracking [61]. ToF provides information about the shape and exact position
of the target in the 3-D world, which is confirmed by radar and provides additional information about the speed of each moving target. However, this
work is not applied to microgesture recognition. In addition, although the NVIDIA institute presents a data-layer fusion HGR method by directly
inputting radar data, RGB camera data, and depth camera data into the DCNN [62], they ignore that the characteristics of measurement data between
sensors, such as the data size, data structure, and unrelated physical attributes, are always different.

The existing research focuses on HGR based on data fusion of wearable devices, radar–vision fusion for autopilot, and multimodal fusion for behavior
tracking, while research on gesture recognition based on radar–vision fusion is still in its early stages. Moreover, existing studies do not provide
sufficient attention to the information fusion of multimodal gesture data. In view of the increasingly complex noncontact HGR scenarios, it is important
to excavate this high-dimensional structural information of multimodal data. In particular, regarding the HGR based on radar–vision fusion, a method
that effectively establishes bridges linking heterogeneous attributes and complementary information between data of different sensors has not been
investigated.

C. Problem Analysis
Related work on vision- and radar- based HGR indicates that although cameras and radar have been used to implement hand gesture capture, cameras
are good at collecting position and morphological information, while radar is good at collecting radial range and velocity information. The two sensors
are highly complementary. On one hand, radar can provide radial information with higher resolution than depth cameras. On the other hand, the RGB
camera can supplement the imaging information and obtain the position at a low cost. Therefore, it is sensible to combine cameras and radar for
gesture acquisition.
The main challenge of HGR is to reliably implement gesture capture and recognition in complex environments. However, it is difficult for RGB cameras
to work in brightly lit or dark environments. In crowded indoor or multiradar deployment scenes, the radar spectrum energy of gestures will be
obscured by environmental clutter, which leads to the loss of the hand object. Therefore, the existing HGR studies based on a single sensor have
difficulty achieving robust HGR in the above complex environments.
Another challenge of HGR is the ambiguity and variability of gestures performed by different people or in different FOV scenes. The habits and hand
size of different humans will cause evident discrepancies in the range and speed of the same gesture. The different relative positions with the
measurement platform will also lead to confusion between the gesture categories. The gesture classification errors caused by these distortions restrict
the generalization performance of HGR.

SECTION IV.
Methodology

Our work achieves dynamic gesture recognition that adapts to complex environment and has generalization performance of individual based on two
aspects. First, according to the displacement and energy differences of each frame of gesture, a preprocessing method is designed to synchronize the
time and structure of vision and radar data. Second, we propose a novel multimodal dynamic gesture recognition method based on a two-branch fusion
deformable network with Gram matching. The input layer of the two branches is replaced by the deformable layer. The spatial features of each moment
are fed into the long short-term memory (LSTM) layer. Gram matrix matching is presented as a loss function to fully exploit the complementary
advantages of multimodal data. An overview of the proposed method is shown in Fig. 1.
A. Time-Synchronized Preprocessing Method
In the application of HGR, dynamic gestures generally consist of the following three stages: the preparation stage, core stage, and retraction stage. The
preparation and retraction stages of different gestures are roughly similar. The core stage contains the main information of gesture motion, such as
different speeds and ranges of movement. Therefore, the extraction of the start and end time-stamps of the core stage is very important for gesture
classification.
However, regarding multisensor data streams, the segmentation of gesture core stage is a difficult task because of the great differences in data structure
and physical characters. Optical-based RGB video data reflect the morphological changes in the hand, while radar-based RDM data contain the
difference in signal energy amplitudes of gestures.

B. Dynamic Deformable Gesture Recognition Network
Although time-synchronized preprocessing integrates the structure of two-stream data, deep fusion is restricted by the irrelevance of physical
properties. Since RGB camera-based optical information and radar-based electromagnetic radiation information cannot be learned by a single network,
a two-branch network structure is used to fully extract the multimodal spatial features of gestures. The two branches have the same structure, and the
input convolutional layers are replaced by deformable convolutions to address the gesture distortion caused by different measurement conditions.
Moreover, since a single CNN cannot exploit the temporal information embedded in the data, combinations of CNN and LSTM units have been used for
temporal dynamic gesture modeling. Hence, we propose a two-branch deformable dynamic HGR network, as shown in Fig. 2.

SECTION VI.
Experiment Analysis and Discussion
In this section, the advantages of the proposed method in complex environment adaptability and generalization are verified. To evaluate the fitting
degree of the training process, accuracy, average recall, average precision, and average F1-score are used as metrics, the initial learning rate is set to 0.1,
and the balance factor is set to 0.01. The entire experiments are implemented in a computer with an Intel i7-8750H processor and an Nvidia Geforce
GTX 1050Ti GPU card.
A. Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation
Due to the differences in the personal habits of the subjects, gesture samples of the same category may express different meanings. Therefore,
satisfactory generalizability to different users is significant for the HGR. We use leave-one-out verification to evaluate the generalization performance of

the proposed method. Regarding each leave-one-out validation, one subject who did not participate in the training was selected as the validation set of
the unknown source, and the remaining seven subjects were selected as the training set. The accuracy, average recall, average F1-score, and average
precision of gesture classification for each subject are provided to evaluate the generalization. Meanwhile, the single-class recognition accuracy of each
gesture is also used to evaluate the difference between gesture categories.
The leave-one-out validation of eight subjects for ten gesture categories is shown in Table III. The proposed method has the best classification
performance for sub.6, with an accuracy of 98.89%, an average precision of 99.09%, an average recall, and an average F1-score of 99%. Similarly, sub.1,
sub.2, sub.3, and sub.8 all achieved above 95% with regards to these four metrics. The result indicates that although personal habits lead to the
ambiguity and variability of gestures, the proposed method has good generalization for gesture classification of different subjects. By averaging the
metrics of each subject, the proposed method achieves a 94.58% accuracy, 94.66% average recall, 94.57% average F1 score, and 95.22% average
precision.
In addition, the recognition results of each gesture category show that the precisions of the six gestures are all above 95%, while only the precision of the
draw-circle gesture is lower than 90%, and the precisions of pull-down and push-forward gestures are below 95% but above 90%. This is because the
proposed method can achieve complementary advantages through the combination of RGB image data and RDM data.
Since camera and radar have different sensitivities to the physical properties, the single transmit and single receive antenna channel of the radar sensor
is used to collect the radial range and velocity of the gestures, while the azimuth, elevation, and imaging information of the gestures are collected by the
camera. The texture features in multimodal data are fully extracted by the loss function of Gram to further improve the representation of features. The
network structure of the proposed method includes deformable convolution, which can enhance the generalization performance through a more flexible
receptive field.
B. Verification of Six Environments
As gesture sensing scenarios expand, robustness has become extremely important for gesture recognition based in complex environments. We deploy
six complex environments, as shown in Fig. 5. The open scene outside the laboratory leads to weak electromagnetic clutter, while a bio-radar for
detecting human vital signs is placed inside the crowded laboratory to simulate measurement conditions with high environmental clutter. Fig. 5(a) and
(b) shows that energy dispersion and redundant targets occur in the RDM of the in-laboratory scene, which decrease the performance of the RDM data.
Similarly, different lighting conditions are used to evaluate the adaptability of the proposed method to illumination. Fig. 5(c) and (d) shows that the
amount of information expressed by the RGB image is sharply decreased by the dark and bright light conditions.
Multimodal and single-modal data are collected by new subjects to implement gesture classification for the six environments. The network structure of
HGR using single-modal data is the proposed method with a single stream. Table IV summarizes the results of HGR for the six environments using
multimodal data of RGB images and RDM. Classification based on multimodal data has achieved 94.63% precision in the out-of-laboratory of normal
light scenes. Under the in-laboratory of normal light scene, recognition performance, which drops slightly, is above 93%. Although electromagnetic

E. Discussion
In this work, we try to address the issue that a single sensor cannot reliably achieve HGR in complex environments and different FOV scenarios. A
multimodal measurement platform composed of radar and camera is used to collect gesture data. The well-designed comparison experiments show the
recognition performance of ten gestures in different environments. The proposed multimodal method achieves relatively stable performance for the six
environments.
Regarding outdoor environments, radar data can always provide support for gesture classification regardless of lighting changes. Regarding indoor
environments, radar performance is degraded by background noise. The weak spectrum in the RDM of individual gestures, such as draw-circle (b) and
click (h), is easily disturbed by indoor background noise. Some gestures that rely on radial features, such as push-forward (a), push-backward (e), and
waving (j), are also affected to varying degrees. In the dark environment, some gestures, such as slide-left (f), slide-right (g), pull-up (I), and pull-down
(c), have a certain deviation in the classification results. In the bright light environment, gestures with a good classification performance also experience
a decrease in accuracy.

Gesture classification results from a single sensor can better reflect the dependence of gesture data from different sensors in complex environments.
From these experiments, it is evident that multimodal data have a high advantage over single sensor data. Collecting data in a variety of environments
and scenarios makes our experimental results rich and convincing. In the future, we will collect more subjects to build a complete multimodal gesture

Our work does not require spatial synchronization since the classified objects are dynamic gestures. In radar data, as the gesture changes, the size and
position of the energy scattering center of the RDM from different frames will change. Regarding vision data, images from different frames can show
displacement variations in gesture key-points. The proposed time synchronization preprocessing method is used to extract and segment the start and
end times of each gesture piece from the multimodal data with different start time stamps. Each slice is labeled according to the gesture items
performed by the subject, thus obtaining camera and radar gesture data with the same starting time stamp.
We deploy the measurement platform with separate radar and camera sensors. First, by adjusting the sampling frequencies of the radar and camera,
the scales of the data generated by the two sensors agree within 1 s. Then, the camera and radar are turned on separately for object detection. After the
gesture acquisition is complete, we turn off both the sensors and derive gesture data with different physical properties. It is worth noting that the input

SECTION VII.
Conclusion
In this article, we propose a novel multimodal dynamic HGR method based on a two-branch fusion deformable network with Gram matching to address
the unreliable HGR caused by complex environments and differences in personal habits. The experimental results indicate that the proposed method
effectively improves the adaptability of the classifier to complex environments and exhibits satisfactory robustness to multiple subjects. Ablation
analysis shows that deformable convolution and Gram loss not only provide reliable HGR but also enhance the generalization ability of classification
tasks in different FOV scenarios. Overall, the experiments show that the proposed radar–vision fusion-based dynamic gesture recognition method is a
robust solution that is applicable to complex environments with strong generalizations.
