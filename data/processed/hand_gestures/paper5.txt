Abstract—The millimeter wave radar-based dynamic gesture 
recognition is the use of electromagnetic waves to obtain the 
motion characteristics of human dynamic gestures, to achieve 
automatic recognition of gestures. This paper proposes an 
frequency modulated continuous wave (FMCW) millimeter wave 
radar gesture recognition technology, the analysis and processing 
of radar echo, after the radar signal detection, in a complex 
environment to extract the gesture and radar distance, speed and 
other features, to get the radar and target azimuth angle, elevation 
angle, distance-weighted doppler, azimuth elevation angle 
covariance, azimuth angle-weighted doppler correlation, elevation 
angle-weighted doppler correlation. The six features are used to 
classify the six gestures based on the features using a shallow 
artificial neural network. It is demonstrated that this method can 
quickly and effectively identify different categories of gestures and 
has good generalisation to untrained test sets.   
Keywords—millimeter wave radar, dynamic gesture recognition, 
convolutional neural networks.  
I. INTRODUCTION 
Human-computer interaction plays an important role in the 
technological life of human beings. Among them, gesture 
interaction has a very broad application prospect in the fields of 
advanced assisted driving and wearable devices due to its natural 
and intuitive characteristics. Existing gesture recognition 
methods include wearable sensors, visual images based on 
visible light and infrared [1], [2], ultrasound, TV, RFID, non-
broadband wireless communication signals such as Wi-Fi [3] 
and radar [4]. However, vision-based gesture recognition 
solutions are highly influenced by lighting conditions [5], while 
wearable sensor-based recognition solutions require additional 
sensors to be worn on the user's hand, which is highly unportable 
and seriously affects the user's experience. Radar-based gesture 
recognition systems do not require additional sensors to be worn 
on the human hand and are not affected by lighting conditions, 
they can work 24/7, and the radar signal has good penetration. 
Compared to gesture recognition systems based on low-band 
wireless signals, millimetre wave radar sensors are easily 
miniaturised and can be embedded inside the device, greatly 
improving its integration and reliability. 
In recent years, radar-based gesture recognition methods have 
attracted a lot of attention [6]-[12]. In the literature [6], Principal 
Component Analysis (PCA) was used to identify gestures and 
the classification results showed that radar-based gesture 
recognition has good potential in the field of smart homes. The 
paper [7] combined data from multiple sensors and used a 
combination of heterogeneous image alignment and a 3D 
Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to identify typical 
gestures in car driving. The paper [8] used a combination of 
heterogeneous image alignment and a three-dimensional 
convolutional neural network to recognize typical hand gestures 
in driving. In the paper [8], micro-doppler features were 
extracted and classified by Deep Convolutional Neural Network 
(DC-NN), and the experimental results showed that the 
recognition accuracy of seven gestures was 93.1%. In [9], a 60 
GHz dedicated millimetre wave radar chip was used to collect 
gestures and extract low-dimensional feature vectors from them, 
and a random forest classifier was used to recognise multiple 
gestures. In [10], a two-stream fusion neural network (TS-FN) 
was used for gesture recognition.  
Stream Fusion Neural Network (TS-FNN) to identify 10 
common gestures in life. The paper [11] extracted the distance-
Doppler-time graph features, distance-time features, Doppler-
time features and horizontal angle-time graph features of the 
gestures and classified the gestures by a light-weighted CNN. 
The gesture actions were classified. 
The FMCW millimetre wave radar is a sensor that can 
estimate the distance, velocity and angle information of a target 
from its echoes. The gesture recognition technology based on 
millimetre wave radar mainly obtains the feature spectrum map 
by extracting the distance, speed and even angle information of 
the gesture action in the echo for estimation. In the existing 
research, most of the gesture recognition methods use the feature 
spectrum map in the form of image dataset using Support Vector 
Machine (SVM) or artificial neural neural network and other 
classifiers for recognition, which is computationally intensive. 
In the Soli project, Google used a millimetre wave radar with a 
bandwidth of up to 7 GHz for gesture recognition, using a 
convolutional neural network to extract frame-by-frame features 
from the distance-Doppler image at successive moments, and a 
recurrent neural network to extract temporal features for gesture 
classification.

II. MILLIMETER WAVE RADAR DETECTION PRINCIPLE 
A. Gesture Signal Pre-processing 
Gesture movement is a process that takes a certain amount 
of time. Determining the observation time of a gesture requires 
two considerations: firstly, the duration of the detection should 
include the actual duration of the gesture movement; secondly, 
the processing time of the algorithm needs to be minimised. The 
IWR6843AOP is a millimetre wave radar device with a sweep 
signal of 128 frames per frame and a frame duration of 40 ms. 
Therefore, the observation time of 25 frames (duration of about 
1 second) was chosen for the analysis of the radar signal during 
the observation time.  
The IF signal obtained from the RF front-end processing is 
sampled by the ADC and the digital signal obtained is done as a 
3D FFT (Fast Fourier Transform) to estimate the distance, speed, 
azimuth and pitch angle of the target. 
B. Gesture Signal Processing 
Firstly, transmit chirp signal. A group of chirps transmitted 
by the FMCW radar, called a "frame" (shown in Fig.1). Doing a 
2D-FFT on the data received by each antenna will result in a 2D 
grid containing the distance and doppler dimensions. A peak in 
the grid indicates the presence of a target at the distance (shown 
in Fig.2). The angle of arrival of the target is obtained by 
processing the corresponding peaks, which means angle-FFT in 
the grid from multiple RX antennas (shown in Fig.3). 
 
Fig. 1. Chirp and frame. 
 
Fig. 2. 2D-FFT Grid. 
 
Fig. 3. Angle-FFT. 
As can be seen from the above diagrams, the two-
dimensional FFT processing process of the FMCW radar signal 
is as follows: the sampled data of the IF signal of each cycle are 
firstly subjected to FFT calculation along the direction of 
sampling, i.e. distance dimension FFT(range), and the result 
obtained is saved to each column of the matrix, which gives the 
distance information of the target; then the FFT calculation of 
the chirp dimension of the matrix, i.e. velocity dimension FFT 
(doppler), which saves the result in the corresponding row of the 
matrix and gives the velocity information of the target; finally, 
the FFT operation is performed on the same frame of data 
obtained from multiple receiving antennas, i.e. angle dimension 
FFT.

III. GESTURE FEATURES 
A. Gesture Feature Extraction 
The six gestures that need to be classified in this paper were 
analysed and found that left slide, upward movement and 
counter-clockwise rotation belong to the more different 
movements, while left slide and right slide belong to two 
movements with a higher degree of similarity. Therefore, 
according to the characteristics of the gestures, the six gestures 
are grouped into three categories. The first category is horizontal 
sliding movements, including slide left and slide right (shown in 
Fig.5). The second category is vertical sliding movements, 
including slide up and slide down (shown in Fig.6), and the third 
category is rotation movements, including counterclockwise 
rotation and clockwise rotation (shown in Fig.7). The following 
is an analysis of the movement characteristics of each gesture.

The energy changes of the reflected signals of the left and 
right sliding manoeuvres are the same, showing an increasing 
and then decreasing trend; the azimuth angles formed by the 
gestures and the radar change in opposite directions; the 
magnitudes of the gesture doppler velocities both become 
smaller and then larger, with the direction going from negative 
to positive. Unlike the left and right slides, the upward and 
downward sliding manoeuvres have a greater variation in the 
pitch angle formed with the radar. In both clockwise and 
counterclockwise rotation movements, the energy, and doppler 
distance magnitudes are constant, and the azimuth and pitch 
angle of the signal need to be considered. In summary, it is 
possible to distinguish the rotation action based on distance and 
Doppler information, and to distinguish counterclockwise and 
clockwise rotation based on the correlation between azimuth and 
pitch angle; up and down sliding and left and right sliding based 
on whether the azimuth angle changes, and finally left and right 
sliding based on azimuth and doppler correlation, and up and 
down sliding based on pitch angle and doppler correlation. 
Based on the motion characteristics of the six gestures, a new 
multi-dimensional feature extraction algorithm is proposed in 
this paper: for each gesture, six types of features are extracted: 
weighted doppler, azimuth, pitch angle, azimuth-pitch angle 
covariance, azimuth-weighted doppler correlation and pitch 
angle-weighted doppler correlation, and a multi-dimensional 
parameter set is constructed as the input to the ANN (artificial 
neural network) classifier. The figure below shows the distance-

B. Gesture Feature Calculation 
1) Azimuth angle 
The angle of deviation of the strongest reflector in front of 
the radar in the horizontal direction is indicated, and the 
horizontal angle of the gesture is detected to distinguish between 
left and right swipe gestures. In the system studied in this paper, 
the number of receiving antennas used is four. The data obtained 
from the four antennas are subjected to a 3D-FFT operation to 
obtain a distance-Doppler-angle map and, based on the peak 
index in the range box, an azimuth angle. 
2) Elevation angle 
It represents the angular offset of the strongest reflector in 
front of the radar in the vertical direction and detects the pitch 
angle of the gesture to distinguish between left- and right-sliding 
gestures, calculated in a similar way to the azimuth calculation. 
Two transmitting antennas are required, and 3D-FFT operations 
are performed from the dimension of the transmitting antennas.

VI. PROBLEMS 
The dynamic gesture recognition system based on millimetre 
wave radar overcomes the limitations of traditional data gloves 
and visual gesture recognition, and has many advantages. The 
use of radar technology for dynamic gesture recognition has 
already achieved certain results, but there are still some 
shortcomings at the technical level. 
A. Simple Gesture Recognition for Short Periods of Time 
As the duration of gesture is 0.5s-0.8s, which is relatively 
short for millimeter wave radar, the gesture is simple and simple, 
and there is a problem of false recognition in the use scenarios 
such as the car. The next research direction is to extend the 
detection time, increase the number of frames, extract more 
complex gestures, recognize and apply them to more complex 
and diverse environments, and provide more user scenarios. 
B. Radar Gesture Recognition in Complex Scenarios 
Compared to the actual complex application environment, 
the experimental scenario of the existing research is relatively 
homogeneous, with millimetre wave radar performing gesture 
detection without too many interfering objects, i.e. a more ideal 
background environment. In practical applications, however, 
due to the uncertainty of the environment, the sample data of 
detected gesture signals are often interspersed with various 
noises. Therefore, future research work can consider setting up 
more complex experimental scenarios or artificially adding 
interference information to ensure that the gesture recognition 
system can maintain a high recognition speed and accuracy rate 
under more complex application scenarios.  
C. Radar Gesture Recognition with Multiple Users 
Compared to independent user gesture recognition, multi-
user radar gesture recognition requires not only the extraction of 
different gesture features and the construction of gesture models, 
but also a rapid and accurate response to different users in the 
same scene. Therefore, the study of multi-user gesture 
recognition in the same scene is a future breakthrough in this 
field. 
VII. CONCLUSION 
In this paper, a millimetre wave radar-based hand gesture 
recognition method is proposed, in which six features, namely
