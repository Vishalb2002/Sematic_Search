ABSTRACT: Liquid metals are highly conductive like metallic
materials and have excellent deformability due to their liquid state,
making them rather promising for flexible and stretchable wearable
sensors. However, patterning liquid metals on soft substrates has
been a challenge due to high surface tension. In this paper, a new
method is proposed to overcome the difficulties in fabricating
liquid-state strain sensors. The method involves adding nickel
powder particles to the liquid metal to maintain the liquid metal’s
fluidity while lowering the surface tension so that the liquid metal
can be easily patterned on a soft substrate using magnets. With the
addition of 12 wt % nickel powder (40 μm) to the liquid metal, a
gauge factor of 5.17 can be achieved at 300% strain. In addition, by
the integration of multiple strain sensors in a smart glove to monitor
14 joints of the human hand, 10 sign language gestures can be recognized by comparing the results of five different machine learning
models, among which the quadratic discriminative analysis model can be accomplished with an accuracy rate of 100%. The
magnetically patterned nickel-containing liquid-metal strain sensors proposed in this study have a wide range of applications in
intelligent soft robots and human−machine interfaces.
KEYWORDS: magnet patterning liquid metal, 14-channel strain sensor, gesture classification, QDA model, machine learning
1. INTRODUCTION
Wearable sensors and flexible medical monitoring are on the
rise1,2 with the development of modern technologies such as
biology,3,4 electronics,5,6 and artificial intelligence.7,8 Wearable
electronics must be lightweight, highly flexible, stretchable,
mechanically strong, and comfortable for humans to wear.9 In
addition, because wearable flexible electronic devices often need
to be placed on limbs with a large amount of movement and a
significant uncertainty in the direction of movement, it is
essential to prepare smart devices that can accurately detect
human motor activities.10 At present, there are five types of
motion capture systems: mechanical,11 electromagnetic,12
acoustic,13 optical,14 and inertial motion capture system.15
Mechanical, electromagnetic, and acoustic motion capture
systems are rarely used in practice due to their limitations in
flexibility and accuracy and the harsh environment required for
electromagnetic waves.16 Optical motion capture systems are
limited to capturing information about human motion posture
in fixed scenes with cameras.17 They are limited to fixed
environments because they require a camera, although they are
the most accurate and mature motion capture technologies. The
inertial motion capture system uses microelectromechanical
system inertial sensors to track the movement of the human
body.18 These sensors measure acceleration and angular velocity
data along the X, Y, and Z axes in a spatial coordinate system to
obtain motion characteristics. Inertial motion capture systems
are subject to signal drift after extended usage. As a result, many
motion capture devices based on flexible sensors have been
developed to increase compatibility with human skin.19
There are four main types of flexible sensors: resistive sensors,
capacitive sensors, piezomagnetic sensors, and inductive
sensors. In previous decades, flexible resistive strain sensors
were made using rigid materials, such as graphene,20−22 carbon
nanotubes,23−25 or silver nanowires,26−28 integrated into softer
elastomers or patterned on softer polymer substrates. The
traditional resistance strain sensor, which uses rigid conductive
materials, is widely applicable. Nevertheless, the development of
flexible and stretchable electronic devices is limited because
these rigid materials increase the elastic modulus of soft
polymers, thereby reducing their stretchability.29
Liquid metal (LM), with eutectic gallium indium (EGaIn)
alloys as the popular trend, is regarded as a popular functional
material that can sense external stimuli, make appropriate

there are some reports on strain sensors for detecting human
hand joints,60,61 they primarily demonstrate sensor signals
relative to joint bending and lack comprehensive detection

elastomers enables LM-based sensors to detect and capture a

There are multiple ways to alter the shape and movement of
liquid metals. These methods include regulating the flow of
liquid metal in microfluidic channels, dispensing, and printing
flows, modifying the surface tension to change fluid dynamics,
and using magnetic forces to manipulate liquid-metal magnetic
particle composites.33 The noncontact magnetic manipulation
method offers precise control of liquid metal and is inexpensive
and easy to operate. This makes it highly promising for soft
robotics and intelligent electronics.
Liquid fabrication of magnetically responsive materials has
been extensively researched since the 1930s. When a liquid
substance is mixed with magnetic particles of a solid, it forms a
ferrofluid with magnetic properties.34,40 Recently, one of the
liquid metals, EGaIn, has a wide range of practical applications in
stretchable resistance sensors due to its high conductivity, low
toxicity, and inherent deformation reversibility.29,35 EGaIn is a
mixture of 75.5% gallium and 24.5% indium. It has a melting
point of 15.7 °C, excellent electrical conductivity of 3 × 106 S/m,
high thermal conductivity, and excellent fluidity. At the same
time, it also has the characteristics of instantaneous oxidation,
which can spontaneously react with oxygen in air to form an
oxide layer with a thickness of 0.7−3 nm,30 which hinders the
further oxidation of LM.36 Liquid metal has high conductivity
and inherent tensile properties. This is an existing problem of
LM, and we share some literatures in Figure S3.31,62−68
Increasing the oxidation of LM by mechanical agitation of the
LM is a process that continually destroys its surface oxide layer
and generates a new oxide layer. Reducing the surface tension of
the liquid metal by stirring the liquid metal to obtain the oxide
layer does not greatly affect the conductivity of the liquid metal.
After 60 min of electromagnetic stirring, the conductivity of the
liquid metal decreased by only 0.4%.69
Many researchers and scholars have prepared stable sensors
using a liquid metal. With the addition of SiO2 to liquid metal, a
flexible resistive sensor was prepared. The sensor has increased
sensitivity and can capture various human activities with a gauge
factor (GF) of 10.3 at 300% strain.29 A stand-alone FS-GaIn
with high conductivity and stability was fabricated with a
conductivity of 5.79 × 105 S/m and a tensile strength of 1350%
by adding silver nanowires to LM. This was achieved by laser
processing and direct etching. However, the sensitivity of this
material was limited due to a small 10% change in resistance.37 A
conductive network of gallium and indium was formed by layer-
by-layer deposition, resulting in an electrical conductivity of 2.37
× 106 S/m. By adjustment of the number of layers of LM during
deposition, the GF of the film can be varied between 0.2 and 1.
This allows its use in artificial skin and energy harvesters.38 In
addition, LM-based magnetic pastes were made by dispersing 15
wt % of 100 nm magnetic particles in the LM matrix. These
pastes can respond rapidly to stimulation by an applied magnetic
field. This also provides excellent ideas for wearable electrodes
and electrosensitive bioelectrodes.39 In addition, other reports
involve liquid-metal technology, such as liquid-metal fibers. An
electrically conductive fiber with 700% strain can be made by
injecting liquid metal into hollow polymer (Karton SEBS)
fibers.41 A coaxial wet-spinning process based on liquid-metal
particles and PVDF-HFP-TF was used for the preparation of LM
sheathing microfibers, which are highly conductive fibers that
can reach 1170% strain. The use of a coaxial wet-spinning
process to fabricate liquid-metal sheath-core microfibers showed
a 15% increase in the change in resistance over 600 cycles at
100% strain.42 Some other papers investigating the stability of
liquid-metal sensors, such as the strain sensors prepared from
silk proteins and hydrogels, showed a 9% increase in the change
in resistance over 2000 cycles at 100% strain.43 Also, the change
in the relative resistance of stretchable sensing systems prepared
from a biphasic liquid-metal particles prepared stretchable
sensing system increased the change in relative resistance by
160% at 500% strain during 5000 cycles.44 Compared to other
papers for the LM fluidity change, strain sensors for detecting
human activity were prepared by adding 10−50 wt % SiO2 to
LM to form a putty state.29 However, in this state, the LM loses
its rheological properties. Besides, deformable composites were
prepared by dispersing eutectic gallium indium tin liquid metal
in silicones, and the fluidity of eutectic gallium indium tin liquid
metal in the cured composites was reduced.45 In addition, paste
slurries prepared by mixing quartz and eutectic Ga−In alloys
have high adhesion and plasticity properties and thus have been
used as printed substrates for the fabrication of soft electronics.
However, at the same time, the fluidity of its liquid-metal alloys
is also reduced.46 Their work focuses on modification of the
liquid metal, which does not help their patterning while reducing
the LM’s fluidity. Stretchable composites were prepared by
mixing liquid metal and a PDMS organosilicon matrix, which
made liquid metal less mobile.57 Therefore, the fluidity of LM
sensors is limited, which restricts their patterning process.
The three organs of the human hand, bones, joints, and
skeletal muscles form the body’s locomotor system in the narrow
sense of the word and help the body perform basic activities. The
bones are the levers of movement; the joints are the fulcrums,
and the skeletal muscles provide the power for movement. The
hand accounts for about 60% of the body’s weight. During body
movement, the joints produce bending strain, and the muscles
undergo pulling and contracting deformations in the process.19
There are many studies that use machine learning to recognize
complex and similar gestures. A wearable glove was prepared by
converting polyester textiles into superhydrophobic textiles
using a CNT/TPE coating.47 The CNN model was also utilized
to recognize gestures such as watering, rotating, and lighting
with an accuracy of 95.23%.47 Despite the high correct rate, it is
limited by the complex signal processing work. Besides, a silk−
gelatin hydrogel smart glove based on 1D-CNN was developed
to achieve gesture language recognition with 99% accuracy.48 In
addition, a sign language gesture recognition system based on
liquid metal and soft silicone was developed by studying 12 static
sign language gesture data sets. The random forest model has the
highest recognition rate of 97.3%, while the linear discriminant
analysis (LDA) model has the lowest recognition rate of
87.4%.49 Moreover, the haptic gloves prepared based on liquid
metal and SiO2 can realize the recognition of boxing techniques
such as jabbing and swinging with 90.5% recognition accuracy
under the CNN model.50 There is also a lot of research related to
signal detection devices for sensors. The hydrogel-based sensor
uses a multiplexer (ADG 1606) to control the collected signals.
This is followed by a charge amplification circuit, a low pass filter

In this paper, magnetic powder dispersions were prepared by
adding nickel powder with a particle size of 40 μm to liquid metal
at different ratios (0−20 wt %). A poly(ethylene terephthalate)
(PET) mask was prepared on the Ecoflex00-30 substrate by laser
cutting, and then the LM was patterned on the laser-cut mask
using a lower-cost magnet. The patterned samples were then
encapsulated using Ecoflex00-30 to obtain wearable skin-like
flexible strain sensors. During the stretching process, the
presence of nickel powder hinders the flow of electrons in the
liquid metal, which leads to a change in the relative resistance.
The GF at 12 wt % nickel powder was 5.17 at 300% strain. After
2000 cycles at 100% strain, the sensor still maintained good
electrical properties and exhibited stability and fatigue
resistance. In addition, the use of patterned liquid metal for
the fabrication of wearable electronics has the advantages of low
limitations, low cost, and high maneuverability. Meanwhile, in
practical applications, the strain sensor made of patterned
magnetic liquid metal can achieve a good fit with different joints
of the finger. The strain sensor can be attached to measure the
activities of 14 joints of the human hand simultaneously. In
addition, the quadratic discriminant analysis (QDA) machine
learning method is utilized to classify the 14-channel strain
sensors for sign language gesture recognition with 100%
accuracy.
2. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
2.1. Preparation Process of Magnet-Induced Pat-
terned Liquid-Metal Strain Sensors. Strain sensors were
prepared using a magnet to pattern liquid metal with different
concentrations of Ni at 0−20 wt %. To fabricate the strain
sensors, a layer of Ecoflex00-30 was first spin-coated on a
PMMA substrate at 300 rpm/60 s and then cured at 80 °C for 10
min. Immediately thereafter, a PET mask was placed on the
cured Ecoflex00-30 and patterned using a laser-cutting device.
Then, Ni-EGaIn droplets were dropped on the patterned PET
mask, the patterning of Ni-EGaIn was completed using a
magnet, and the PET mask was removed after patterning. The
patterned sample was attached with copper tape to complete the
electrode connection. The moisture vapor spray dressing
process creates a transparent film on the surface of the
conductive path after curing so that the LM does not spill
outside the path. This step secures and reinforces the connection
between the conductive path and substrate. Diagrams of the
sensors with and without moisture vapor spray dressing during
stretching are shown in Figure S1. The LM at the electrode
position without moisture vapor spray dressing has leakage
under deformation, but the sensor with moisture vapor spray
dressing performs well under the same strain. After that, a layer
of Ecoflex00-30 was spin-coated on it at 300 rpm/60 s and cured
at 80 °C for 10 min. A layer of Ecoflex-gel was spin-coated onto
the cured sample at 600 rpm/60 s for bonding with human
hands and cured at 80 °C for 30 min. Finally, the cured samples
were peeled off from the PMMA. So, the liquid metal flows only
within the formed conductive channels. The preparation process
is shown in Figure 1. The specific preparation methods are
discussed in detail in the Experimental Section. Ferrofluids with
magnetic properties are created by mixing nickel powder with a
liquid metal. When there is strain, the presence of nickel powder
particles in the liquid metal can impede its flow. The resistance
change under different strains allows for health monitoring
during various human activities.
When different proportions of nickel powder are added to the
liquid metal, the powder floats on the surface due to the high
surface tension of the liquid. Liquid metals are difficult to diffuse
on solid surfaces due to their high surface tension. Figure 2a is a
physical diagram of the prepared liquid-metal strain sensor.
Using a glass rod for stirring, more nickel powder will be coerced
into the liquid metal by the oxide layer of the liquid metal, and
the mixture continues to stir to make the nickel powder
completely and uniformly enter the liquid metal. The specific
mixing and stirring process is shown in Figure 2b.
Liquid metal with different ratios of nickel powder is
patterned directly onto an Ecoflex soft substrate with a PET
film using a low-cost rubidium iron−boron magnet (NdFeB) (B
= 0.6 T), as shown in Figure 2c. When an NdFeB magnet is
positioned beneath the Ecoflex00-30 soft substrate, the
magnetic field pulls the magnetic particles in the liquid metal

Liquid-metal strain sensors containing nickel particles show
considerable potential for human wearable devices because of
their high flexibility and stretchability, high sensitivity to small
and large strains, wide strain sensing range, and excellent
durability. By spin-coating a layer of Ecoflex-gel on the strain
sensor, it can be attached to different parts of the human skin,
such as the wrist, elbow, neck, side neck, and other human body
positions, so that various movements can be detected. As shown
in Figure 6, when the sensor is attached to the wrist joint, a
relative resistance curve can be observed when the wrist is bent
and straightened (Figure 6a), and the change in relative
resistance increases from 0% to 17.5% when the sensor is
attached to the elbow joint (Figure 6b). At the same time, the
strain sensor also detects the change in relative resistance when
the neck is bent and twisted (Figure 6c,d). The ability of liquid-
metal sensors to accurately record the bending and relaxation of
various joints in the human body makes liquid-metal sensors
valuable in the future for smart robots and interactive wearable
electronic devices.
2.4. Sign Language Gesture Recognition Using Multi-
channel Liquid-Metal Sensors. Figure 7a shows the
flowchart of sign language gesture recognition. To fully digitize
the finger gestures and recognize the sigh language gesture
information, 14 LM strain sensors corresponding to the finger
joints were connected to the 14 corresponding joints of the test
finger using 3M tape for point-to-point detection. The human
hand has 14 finger joints. These 14 finger joints include five
metacarpophalangeal joints, five proximal phalangeal joints, and
four distal phalangeal joints. Among them, the thumb has only
one proximal phalangeal joint, in addition to the metacarpo-
phalangeal joint, as shown in Figure 7c. The acquisition of data
from the 14 joints is realized by pasting the 14-channel strain
sensors to the hand joints. The sensors and peripheral signal
detection devices of the 14-channel used are shown in Figure 7b.
The strain sensor is connected to the PCB via copper wires; 14
voltage dividing resistors are soldered to the PCB, and ESP 32
PICO D4 is inserted in the corresponding position. Because the
strain sensor is a resistive change, only the resistors and the strain
sensor are needed for voltage dividing to convert the resistance
signal into a voltage signal to read by the ADC port of the MCU.
Therefore, only 14 resistors are required for the peripheral
circuit. ESP 32 PICO D4 is chosen as the MCU model because it
contains enough ADC interfaces to read the 14-channel
synchronized signals. Figure S5 shows the real-time evaluation
of the relative resistance rate of change of the nickel-containing
LM strain sensors for 5 channels under 10 gestures. The real
response process of each joint bending was recorded by the real-
time change of the resistance of 14 strain sensors doing 10
different gestures (1−10), as shown in Figure 7d. Different
gestures have different finger-bending angles, which can improve
the accuracy of the gesture recognition.
The voltage signals collected by 14-channel and 5-channel
strain sensors for 10 sign language gestures are analyzed using
five machine learning models to realize strain sensors with
intelligent perceptual recognition and feedback functions. The
five models used here are QDA, LDA, extreme gradient boosting
(XGBoost), linear support vector classifier (Linear-SVC), and
stochastic gradient descent (SGD). The sensing area was
designed with a length of 15 mm, so that the position of the
sensors with the corresponding joints can be adjusted for
different individuals during data collection. Due to the length
and flexibility of different individuals, different strain and
resistance changes occurred between individuals at the time of
data acquisition. Therefore, we calibrated the gesture signals by
two different gesture states: fully open and clenched fist. Figures
S6a,b and S7a,b show diagrams before and after calibration
signals for five subjects using the 5-channel and 14-channel
sensors, respectively, to perform the sign language gesture “10”.
Thus, the signals of different individuals after calibration are
comparable. Therefore, the differences in the body length and
flexibility of different individuals do not affect the accuracy of
machine learning. The correctness results of machine learning
for five different models with 5 and 14 channels are shown in
Figure S8. The QDA model has higher accuracy than the other
machine learning models, and the correct rate of gesture
recognition for the 14-channel strain sensor is higher than that of
the 5-channel one. QDA is a classical classification model that
takes into account the category covariance, allowing each
category to have an independent covariance matrix.55,56 LDA
shows lower classification accuracy than QDA, and the main
difference is in how the covariance matrixes are classified and
handled. The nonlinearity of the sensors reduced their accuracy.
QDA assumes that each class has its covariance matrix, and this
discrepancy allows for the soft sensor of nonlinearity to be
explained. The ideal model is a QDA for this application. A
learning curve can be used to describe the stability of the model.
In general, machine learning setting and statistics, learning
curves represent the generalization performance of the model as
a function of the size of the training set.58 As shown in Figures

inconsistency of the data leads to a decrease in the training
scores; therefore, the blue line shows the decreasing results. For
the 14-channel learning curve, as the number of training samples
increases, it is easier for the QDA model to learn the features and
models of the data, thus improving the training score. It can also

3. CONCLUSION
In conclusion, a strain sensor with high mechanical stability,
wide tensile strain range, high sensitivity, and high mechanical
strength was prepared by patterning liquid metal using magnets
and encapsulating it with Ecoflex00−30. The prepared strain
sensor has a GF value of 5.17 at 300% strain and excellent
durability after 2000 cycles at 100% strain. In addition, by
installing 14-channel strain sensors on 14 joints of the human
hand, the motion of 14 joints of the human body can be
detected. The sensing device enables the recognition of 10
different gestures simultaneously, demonstrating the capability
of the bending sensor in gesture recognition and digitization of
different joint motions of the human body. By using the QDA
machine learning, the 14-channel smart glove recognizes 1−10
different gestures with 100% accuracy, which is higher than the
recognition accuracy of the 5-channel system for the same task
(93%). The accuracies of the 14-channel sign language gesture
recognition under the LDA, EGBoost, Linear-SVC, and SGD
models are 88%, 99%, 91%, and 56%, respectively. This work
lays a good foundation for liquid-metal strain sensors to play an
important role in human digitization and provides a good
learning platform for smart wearable systems.
4. EXPERIMENTAL SECTION
4.1. Raw Materials. Eutectic gallium indium (EGaIn) metal alloy
(74.5 wt % Ga and 25.5 wt % In) was purchased from Dingtai Metal
Alloys Co. It remains liquid at room temperature (melting point of 15.7
°C). Nickel particles (99%) were purchased from CMT Xindun Alloy
Co. The average particle size of the near-spherical, high-purity nickel
powder particles was 40 μm (300−500 mesh). Ecoflex00-30
(Smoothing-on Inc., Macungie, PA) is an easy-to-use, platinum-
catalyzed silica gel. Ecoflex-gel (Smoothing-on Inc., Macungie, PA) is a
platinum-catalyzed silicone that is tacky when cured. The NdFeB
magnets used for patterning were purchased from Rongxinyuan
Magnetics, with a magnetic force of 6000 GS and a magnet size of 37
mm × 25 mm. Poly(methyl methacrylate) (PMMA) sheets were
purchased from Qinfen Processing Customization Co., Ltd., and the
size of the acrylic sheet was 90 mm × 20 mm × 2 mm. The PET masks
have a thickness of 0.1 mm and were purchased from Shiwei Materials
Co., Ltd. The PET templates were cut by using a ULS System VL4.60
laser cutter machine.
4.2. Preparation Process of the Ni-EGaIn Sensor Induced by
NdFeB. The PMMA sheets of size 90 mm × 20 mm × 2 mm were
cleaned with deionized water and alcohol, respectively, and then dried
with an air gun. Then, parts A and B of Ecoflex00-30 were mixed in a 1:1
ratio and stirred with a glass rod for about 1 min to mix evenly, and the
air bubbles in the Ecoflex were drained by pumping air in the vacuum
reactor for 5−10 min. Spin coating was performed at a rotational speed
of 300 rpm for 60 s using a spin coater, and the obtained samples were
dried on a hot stage at 80 °C for 10 min.
The PET film with a size of 90 mm × 20 mm × 0.1 mm was attached
to the cured Ecoflex surface, and the desired template shape was cut on
the surface of the PET film by laser cutting. The optimized laser cutting
parameters are a laser power of 6% and a laser cutting speed at 22%.
This allows the masking plate to be cut without damaging the Ecoflex
substrate.
Magnetic particle dispersions were prepared by adding 40 μm
particles of nickel powder as a filler material in liquid metal, and the
amounts of nickel powder were 1, 3, 6, 10, 12, 14, 15, and 20 wt %,
respectively. The two were stirred in a beaker for 10 min until
completely mixed. A drop of the mixed sample was pipetted and placed
on a laser-cut PET mask, and then the liquid metal was patterned with a
rubidium iron−boron magnet with a magnetic force of 6000 GS. The
patterned sample was spin-coated with Ecoflex00-30 at 300 rpm for 60 s
and then dried on a hot stage at 80 °C for 10 min. Ecoflex-gel was spin-
coated on cured Ecoflex00-30 at 600 rpm for 60 s and then dried on a
hot platform at 80 °C for 30 min. This allows the strain sensor to be
attached to human skin.
4.3. Characterization of the Liquid Metal Strain Sensor. In
order to characterize the morphology of liquid metals with different
amounts of nickel powder and ensure the uniformity of nickel powder
addition, the morphology of the prepared nickel-based liquid metals
was characterized by field-emission scanning electron microscopy (FEI
Quanta 450 FEG SEM) and EDS spectral mapping was performed
using an AZtech Energy X-Max 20 EDS system. The tensile properties
of the prepared strain sensors were tested using an Instron 5942 micro
tester, in which 2000 cycles were tested at 100% strain. The

and perform each gesture naturally during acquisition of the gesture

performing a gesture for 3 s, and then repeating the next gesture 10
consecutive times. Five subjects participated in the collection of 10 sign
language gestures representing number 1−10. A total of 1500 samples
were collected, 150 samples for each gesture, of which 1200 samples of
the collected gestures were used as the training data and 300 samples
were used as the test data. In each experiment, a gesture was executed
and lasted for 3 s after remaining stationary for 3 s. The QDA model
used 80% of the data for each gesture as training data, and the remaining
20% of the data was used as the data set.

Learning. In order to categorize sign language gesture data, we
collected 5-channel and 14-channel strain sensors. We utilized Python
3.9.13 to perform machine learning on the data collected by liquid-
metal sensors to determine the accuracy of gesture recognition. The
Python application programming interfaces used are Scikit-learn and
XGBoost, and five models are from each of the two Python APIs, QDA,
LDA, Linear-SVC, SGD, and XGBoost. The first four of these models

parameters used are default values, where the random seed for the
QDA, SGD, and XGBoost models is 353.
