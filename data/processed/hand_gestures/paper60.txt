Abstract—Human Activity Recognition (HAR) possesses 
significant potential in pattern recognition, machine learning, 
wearable computing, computer vision, and notably in 
rehabilitation assistance. With the ability to automatically track 
patients' recovery progress, it addresses the issue of insufficient 
human resources. Presently, improving recognition accuracy 
hinges on the fusion of multiple information sources like 
physiological signals, motion sensors, and visual sensors. In this 
study, a multi-stage multimodal fusion strategy based on 
stacked integration with Deep Convolutional Neural Network 
(DCNN) is proposed. Firstly, the inertial sensor and depth 
sensor data are converted into image data, and the data 
enhancement method is used to extract edge information using 
Prewitt, and features are extracted and spliced by the DCNN to 
realize multimodality at the feature layer. At the decision layer, 
the two unimodal decisions are fused using the stacking fusion 
strategy to obtain the final classification decision. The 
experimental results show that this strategy exhibits significant 
advantages in human motion recognition, with accuracy rates of 
99.85% and 99.51% on Kinect V2 and UTD-MHAD datasets, 
respectively, exceeding the state-of-the-art deep learning 
methods on the same dataset. 
Keywords—Sensors Fusion; DCNN; Rehabilitation Robots; 
Human Activity Recognition 
I. INTRODUCTION  
As the diseases' onset age lowers, hemiparesis becomes 
increasingly common in cardiovascular, cerebrovascular, and 
neurological conditions[1]. Despite the growing importance 
of regular rehabilitation, medications and surgery do not fully 
restore function in hemiplegic patients, and prolonged 
immobilization can lead to complications.With rehab 
resources lacking in China, much demand remains unmet, 
highlighting the relevance of a comprehensive rehabilitation 
robot system. This system would interpret the user's 
movement intentions and physical conditions, relay real-time 
feedback to the controller, and deliver essential assistance. 
Human motion intention recognition technology within this 
system optimizes user-robot synergy and comfort. The 
accuracy of this recognition significantly affects the robot's 
functionality and user experience, hence studying it bears 
considerable significance. 
Current human motion recognition techniques are mainly 
divided into visual and wearable sensor methods. Visual 
sensors, located within specific environments, include a 
variety of cameras like RGB-D, infrared, time-of-flight, 
multispectral, sprite, and thermal imaging. They recognize 
activities through pictures or surveillance videos with the help 
of image and video analysis technologies. The advent of cost-
effective color depth detection devices, for example, 
Microsoft Kinect and Asus Xtion-Pro Live RGB-D 
cameras[2], has eased the acquisition of 3D and depth data. 
Depth sensor's proficiency for joint tracking has intrigued 
numerous computer vision researchers. For instance, 
Patwary[3] used multimodal RGBDT video data and a semi-
supervised learning algorithm, FSSL-PAR, for bedridden 
patient activity recognition. The FSSL-PAR algorithm not 
only effectively recognized patient activities, but also 
substantially 
improved 
the 
classifier's 
generalization 
capability, 
outperforming 
other 
supervised 
learning 
algorithms. Batool et al.[4] proposed a K-Ary entropy 
classifier 
algorithm 
based 
on 
Probabilistic 
Boolean 
Incremental Learning (PBIL). The experiments employed 
depth data and RGB images of three public datasets, and 
utilized 2.5D point cloud modeling and ridge extraction 
techniques to extract full-body features. The results revealed 
a high accuracy of 95.05%, 95.56%, and 95.08%. While visual 
sensors recognize movements through image analysis, they 
face equipment or environment-related challenges. In contrast, 
wearable sensors, which work well in large spaces and start 
functioning when the wearer moves, are more flexible and 
thus commonly used in Human Activity Recognition 
(HAR).A new strategy was proposed [5] for constructing an 
information 
semantic 
space 
based 
on 
IMU(Inertial 
Measurement Unit) data for human activity recognition. The 
experiments incorporated a combination method of video-
based and word-based semantic spaces to improve the zero-
shot performance in human activity recognition using IMU 
data. The results suggested a significant potential in enhancing 
activity recognition tasks using IMU data, with the aid of 
videos of human activities and the related models developed 
in the computer vision space. 
Systems solely using inertial sensors face limits such as 
electromechanical delay and random drift interference, which 
may reduce their precise activity recognition capabilities. 
Merging various types of sensors, like inertial ones prone to 
drift, with more resilient ones can offset these issues,

(HAR) using mixed data has become popular, employing 
techniques such as probability, statistics, machine learning, 
and especially effective deep learning algorithms for  
automatic feature representation.In Bangaru[6] et al.'s study, 
EMG and inertial data were transmitted via Bluetooth from

In this study, a fusion strategy grounded on the multi-
modality concept within the Stacking framework is introduced 
to explore the advantages of multi-modal fusion in human 
motion recognition. The primary achievements of the study 
are outlined below: 
(1) A deep multimodal stacking fusion framework with a 
multilevel structure is employed for effective integration of 
diverse modalities. This ensures the full preservation and 
utilization of the uniqueness of each modality and enhances 
motion recognition accuracy. 
(2) DCNN network model is utilized for data feature 
extraction and feature stitching experiments on different 
layers, providing robust support for subsequent multimodal 
fusion. 
(3) Experiments on public datasets demonstrate that the 
proposed stacking multimodal fusion surpasses other 
algorithms in recognition accuracy, underlining its superior 
performance.

of the proposed method, experimental results, and a 
conclusion. 
II. METHODS 
The 
multimodal 
motion 
recognition 
procedure 
encompasses three parts: preprocessing of data, extraction of 
features, and fusion of sensors. Preprocessing involves data 
enhancement, conversion of sequence data into signal images, 
and edge feature extraction via Prewitt filters. Deep learning 
is exploited in the second stage to process sensor data, and 
concatenate extracted features. The ultimate phase utilizes a 
layered fusion technique for classification, where two 
unimodal features are run through a basic classifier and then a 
meta-classifier to yield the final motion recognition outcomes.

manual means, which often ignores possible correlations 
between different signals, and the data volume is more 
dimensional, which makes the processing workload larger. In 
this study, we first convert the complex multidimensional raw 
sequence data into more easily processed image data. For the

obtain  image data that can reflect edge features(Fig.5). This 
is beneficial as these features can be complementary and 
distinctive during the subsequent feature extraction phase. 
Considering the limited dataset and interference in signal 
acquisition, we augment the depth sensor and IMU sensor data 
to optimize the DCNN model training. For the depth sensor 
image data(Fig.4), we use rotation for data enhancement to 
accommodate environmental instabilities such as sensor 
inversion. For inertial sensor data(Fig.2 and Fig.3), we use a 
dithering strategy to introduce tiny random noise, including 
adding a certain amount of noise to the sensor readings and 
Gaussian noise, thus improving the model's resistance to 
interference. 
B. Single-Modality Feature Extraction  
1)  Establishment and Training of DCNN Model 
DCNN is a traditional CNN(Convolutional Neural 
Network) that adds depth to the network. This means that 
DCNNs are able to extract and learn more complex and deeper 
features. Deeper neural networks typically have better 
performance for solving complex tasks, but also require 
greater computational resources and more sophisticated 
training strategies. 
The architecture of the DCNN utilized in this study, 
illustrated in Fig.7, fundamentally consists of four 
convolutional layers, four pooling layers, and two dense 
layers.The number of kernel filters in each convolutional layer 
is sequential: 32, 64, 64, and 128, and the size of each filter is 
3x3. After every convolutional layer, we have set a Rectified 
Linear Unit(ReLU) activation function layer and a Batch 
Normalization layer, followed by a pooling layer with a 2x2 
kernel and a stride of 2. Batch Normalization's inclusion 
expedites model training, standardizes input layer data, 
improves layer-specific parameter learning, and accelerates 
network learning. After the pooling layers, we added two fully 
connected layers: one with 256 neurons and the other with the 
same number as the activity types in the dataset.The first layer 
maps high-level features into 256-dimensional space, while 
the second maps these to the output space. A softmax 
activation layer then generates category scores and classifies 
results. ReLU is used in training for input and hidden layers, 
addressing the vanishing gradient issue better than sigmoid 
and tanh. These enhancements boost the DCNN model's 
classification accuracy. 
2) Feature concatenation 
In the context of neural network applications, feature 
fusion typically involves extracting feature vectors from 
various layers or regions within the network. These vectors are 
then assembled to compile a more comprehensive feature 
vector. In the section related to feature concatenation, our 
main task focuses on the processing of feature data extracted 
via the DCNN. Firstly, attention is given to the Inertial 
Measurement Unit (IMU) data, which is either processed with

In DCNN, determining which layers of features should be 
fused is a critical step. Since the fusion of higher-level features 
helps to obtain more representative and descriptive features, 
we consider selecting from two fully connected layers for 
feature extraction and splicing. In addition, the Pearson 
correlation coefficient is used to evaluate and select the 
selected network layers with the following formula: 
 
corr(X,Y) =  cov(X,Y)/(std(X)*std(Y)) 
(1) 
X and Y are fully connected layer features of a single 
modality, std(X) and std(Y) denote the standard deviation of 
X and Y, and cov(X, Y)is their covariance. Correlation 
coefficients with absolute values greater than 0.7, less than 0.3,  
or in between represent strong, weak, or moderate associations 
of the features, respectively. 
C. Multi-modal Fusion for Action Recognition 
This study proposes a deep multi-level feature fusion 
framework (Figure 6). The framework uses independent 
convolutional neural networks to process different modalities 
of data and employs multi-branch single-modal data 
concatenation to obtain more complementary features, 
thereby improving classification accuracy. Additionally, it 
utilizes a stack-based fusion strategy for multimodal fusion. 
This strategy includes two types of models: base models and 
meta-models. In this experiment, Random Forest, Support 
Vector Machine (SVM), and k-nearest Neighbors (KNN) are 
selected as meta-models because they have been proven to 
produce 
excellent 
overall 
performance 
in 
other 
experiments[10], and their performance in HAR tasks 
surpasses other classifiers. Specifically, we perform separate 
dual-branch processing for the depth dataset and the inertial 
sensor dataset, each using a neural network trained with a 
DCNN model. In one branch, we first utilize Prewitt filters for 
edge detection processing, then incorporate data enhancement 
techniques such as rotation and perturbation to augment the 
data and improve the robustness and generalization of the 
model, and finally feed the processed data into the network. 
Finally, the features obtained from the training of the two 
branches are spliced. Similarly, the inertial sensor dataset is 
processed in the same way. Subsequently, we perform 
classification prediction on the spliced deep features and 
inertial sensor features by the base model, respectively. 
Eventually, the predictions are fed into a meta-classifier to 
produce the final prediction.  
III. EXPERIMENTS 
A. Experimental Setup 
The Kinect V2 action dataset used in the experiments is a 
publicly available dataset containing depth and inertial sensor 
data. The dataset consists of 10 actions performed by 6 
subjects, with each subject repeating the action 5 times. The 
labels of these 10 actions can be known from the horizontal 
and vertical coordinates in the Fig12. In the experiment, we 
randomly divide the dataset into training data and test data in 
a 9:1 ratio. This ensures there is sufficient data for the model 
to learn various patterns and features, while also providing 
enough data for a preliminary estimate of model performance. 
Since the IMU sensor data is relatively homogeneous with 
insufficient features and a small amount of data, the IMU 
sequences are converted to images and processed using 
dithered data enhancement methods to prevent overfitting. 
Similarly, in order to increase the generalization ability of the 
trained model, the depth sensor data is processed using rotated 
data enhancement techniques. In addition, to generate 
additional features, we use Prewitt edge detection filtering for 
both IMU and depth data, which enriches the spliced features 
and contributes to the subsequent multimodal fusion 
classification accuracy.The platform used for the experiments 
is MATLAB 2020b, and the hardware resource is GPU.

2)  Comparative Experiment on Different Classifier 
Combinations 
In the selection of classifiers, this experiment uses SVM, 
KNN and Random Forest as either meta-classifiers or base 
classifiers. The goal is to compare the recognition accuracy 
under different classifier combinations and thus select the 
optimal scheme. The experiment initially applies KNN, SVM, 
and Random Forest classifiers for single-modal feature 
classification. As per the experimental results, the KNN 
classifier exhibits the best recognition effect for IMU features, 
reaching up to 94.8%. The SVM classifier excels in 
recognizing Depth features, achieving 98.31%, but its 
performance on IMU features is subpar. Conversely, the 
Random Forest classifier performs well on both Depth and 
IMU features, with classification accuracy rates hitting 88.9% 
and 97.87%, respectively. 
Considering the classification effect of the above 
unimodal features, the following three main combination 
schemes are considered for the classifier of multimodal fusion 
for Stacking: 
• Combination 1: KNN is used for IMU features, SVM 
is used for Depth features, and Random Forest is used 
as the meta-classifier. 
• 
Combination 2: KNN is used for both IMU and Depth 
features, and Random Forest is used as the meta-
classifier. 
• 
Combination 3: Random Forest is used for IMU 
features, SVM is used for Depth features, and KNN is 
used as the meta-classifier.

Combination 1 (Fig.11) for the recognition of ten kinds of 
movements has the largest values on the diagonal, 
representing the maximum number of positive instances and 
the highest accuracy of motion recognition, reaching 99.71%.

3)  Comparison Experiment of Multi-modal and Single-
modal Recognition 
The main objective of this section is to explore the 
differences in feature recognition between multi-modal and 
single-modal approaches. The experiment compares the 
recognition accuracy on IMU and depth sensor data and tests 
the effects of data augmentation and Prewitt features 
concatenation in improving recognition accuracy. At the same 
time, it observes and compares whether data fusion could 
further improve the accuracy of recognition. In order to further 
verify the robustness of the algorithm, other datasets are used 
for validation, and comparisons are made with past studies 
that used the same datasets for human motion recognition. 
From the experimental results(Table Ⅱ), it can be seen that 
for the Kinect V2 dataset, the recognition accuracy of our 
proposed unimodal training model is 73.83% on a single IMU 
data.When data enhancement is performed on the IMU data, 
the model recognition accuracy is improved to 94.02%.After 
using data enhancement, splicing preprocessing technique, 
and the KNN classifier, the recognition accuracy of the IMU 
data is further improved to 94.8%. Our proposed unimodal 
training model on single-depth sensor data has a recognition 
accuracy of 96.24%. When the depth data is subjected to data 
augmentation, the recognition accuracy improves to 97.26%. 
after using data augmentation, splicing preprocessing 
technique, and SVM classifier, the recognition accuracy of the 
depth data further improves to 98.38%. Finally, after 
performing our proposed Stacking multi-sensor fusion model 
on the entire dataset, the recognition accuracy is further 
improved to 99.85%. 
To verify the effectiveness of the model, we applied our 
proposed Stacking multi-modal fusion framework to the 
UTD-MHAD[16] dataset. The final classification recognition 
accuracy was 99.51%, and the resulting classification 
confusion matrix is shown in Fig.12. The table presents the 
fusion framework's inference rates for two datasets in 
microseconds(ms), indicating the time required for the 
classifier to process each test sample. 
This experiment demonstrates the considerable advantage 
of the proposed multimodal recognition framework in 
accuracy, in comparison to unimodal recognition. The 
addition of data augmentation and feature concatenation 
techniques further strengthens the framework's recognition 
accuracy. Notably, this framework surpasses the accuracy of 
its predecessor, marking a substantial enhancement. 
IV. CONCLUSION 
In this paper, we innovatively propose a human motion 
recognition method using a deep multimodal stacked 
integrated fusion framework, where the raw sequence data of 
KinectV2 is converted into images and the model is optimized 
by data augmentation and the addition of Prewitt filtering 
features.A DCNN network model is used to extract and fuse 
features from preprocessed unimodal data. Experimental 
results clearly show that our model exhibits high efficiency in 
extracting discriminative features. Our chosen stacking fusion 
strategy uses SVM, KNN, and Random Forest as fusion 
classifiers, and different combinations of classifiers were 
evaluated to determine the optimal model. Subsequently, we 
perform performance tests on the KinectV2 and UTD-MHAD 
datasets. The results show that for the same dataset, the 
recognition accuracy of our proposed Stacking multimodal 
fusion exceeds that of other algorithms, highlighting the 
superior performance of our approach.
