Abstract
Hand skeleton-based gesture recognition is a crucial task in human-computer interaction and virtual reality. It aims to achieve
precise classification by analyzing the spatio-temporal dynamics of skeleton joints. However, existing methods struggle to effec-
tively model highly entangled spatio-temporal features and fuse heterogeneous Joint, Bone, and Motion (J/B/JM) modalities.
These limitations hinder recognition performance. To address these challenges, we propose an Adaptive Spatio-Temporal Net-
work (ASTD-Net) for gesture recognition. Our approach centers on integrated spatio-temporal feature learning and collabora-
tive optimization. First, for spatial feature learning, we design an Adaptive Multi-Subgraph Convolution Module (AMS-GCN)
which mitigates spatial coupling interference and enhances structural representation. Subsequently, for temporal feature learn-
ing, we introduce a Multi-Scale Dilated Temporal Fusion Module (MD-TFN) that captures multi-granularity temporal patterns,
spanning local details to global evolution. This allows for comprehensive modeling of temporal dependencies. Finally, we pro-
pose a Self-Supervised Spatio-Temporal Channel Adaptation Module (SSTC-A). Using a temporal discrepancy loss, SSTC-A
dynamically optimizes cross-modal dependencies and strengthens alignment between heterogeneous J/B/JM features, enhancing
their fusion. On the SHREC’17 and DHG-14/28 datasets, ASTD-Net achieves recognition accuracies of 97.50% and 93.57%,
respectively. This performance surpasses current state-of-the-art methods by up to 0.50% and 1.07%. These results verify the
effectiveness and superiority of our proposed method.

1. Introduction
Hand skeleton-based gesture recognition is crucial for human-
computer interaction, with vast potential in virtual reality, aug-
mented reality, and barrier-free communication [TGHW21, BH93,
ZCCL18]. The core task is designing models to process 3D skele-
ton sequences and infer gesture intentions [SWV∗17, LWL21].
This requires models precisely capturing dynamic spatial structure
and understanding complex temporal motion patterns. Develop-
ing accurate, robust, real-time systems is vital for intuitive human-
computer interaction [QWDJ22].
Research in skeleton-based gesture recognition has evolved from
early hand-crafted feature engineering to deep learning methodolo-
gies. Prominent deep learning techniques in this area include Con-
volutional Neural Networks (CNNs), Recurrent Neural Networks
(RNNs) and their variants (e.g., Gated Recurrent Units (GRU),
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)), and more recently devel-
oped Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs). Deep learning sig-

2. Related work on skeleton-based gesture recognition
2.1. Skeleton-based Gesture Recognition Methods
Early research in skeleton-based gesture recognition primarily re-
lied on hand-crafted features. For instance, Oreifej [OL13] pro-
posed the Histogram of Oriented 4D Normals (HON4D) method
for basic activity recognition. Although capable of capturing shape
and motion information well within depth sequences, it exhibited
shortcomings in utilizing skeletal structure and characterizing fine-
grained hand motion details. To address this, De Smedt [DSWV16]
extracted geometric skeletal features, such as joint angles, to de-
scribe pose variations, enhancing pose characterization capability.
However, this method lacked robustness, being susceptible to view-
point and background changes, thus limiting its practical appli-
cation scope. Subsequently, De Smedt and Wannous [DSWV19]
improved generalization ability by fusing multiple features and
classifiers. Yet, this approach suffered from low computational ef-
ficiency, making it difficult to meet real-time requirements. To
balance performance and efficiency, Deng [DGJY23] proposed a
multi-feature, multi-stream network. It reduced the parameter count
and enhanced feature representation by combining various joint
features with one-dimensional CNNs. However, its recognition per-
formance remained limited in complex dynamic scenarios, particu-
larly those involving rapid gestures and intricate backgrounds.
Recently, deep learning methods have surpassed traditional ap-
proaches in skeleton-based gesture recognition due to their pow-
erful automatic feature learning capabilities. These methods are
primarily categorized into two classes: those emphasizing tempo-
ral dynamics and focusing on spatial modeling. Methods focus-
ing on temporal dynamics primarily employ RNN variants (e.g.,
LSTM, GRU) and Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCNs). For
instance, Du [DWW15] proposed a hierarchical RNN partition-
ing skeletons into segments, while Shin and Kim [SK20] in-
troduced a partitioned GRU-RNN for block processing of fea-
tures. Kim [SKR17] utilized TCNs in an interpretable model, and
Hou [HWC∗18] incorporated temporal attention into TCNs to cap-
ture complex temporal information. Methods focusing on spatial
structure modeling mostly employ CNN frameworks. A common
approach, used by Tu [TLL18], maps skeleton sequences onto
pseudo-images or voxels for CNN processing. However, this trans-
formation to Euclidean space disrupts the skeleton’s native topol-
ogy and connectivity, potentially losing vital structural information.
Furthermore, Liu [LLW∗20] proposed a strategy for separately ex-
tracting spatial and temporal features. Yet, their spatial processing
doesn’t directly handle the native graph structure, limiting effective
spatial structure utilization.
In summary, methods relying solely on temporal or spatial mod-
eling face limitations in fully exploiting the inherent non-Euclidean
graph structure of skeleton data. This constrains the model’s abil-
ity for in-depth exploration of intrinsic data regularities and intri-
cate inter-joint relationships. With their unique ability to perform
computations directly on graph structures and effectively preserve
topological information, GCNs provide a key solution for overcom-
ing this bottleneck and have gained widespread attention from re-
searchers in the field.

Given the demonstrated effectiveness of GCNs in processing
graph-structured data and their inherent suitability for model-
ing the non-Euclidean nature of skeletons, GCN-based methods
have emerged as a prominent research paradigm in skeleton-based
gesture recognition. Li [LHY∗19] proposed the Spatio-Temporal
Graph Convolutional Network (ST-GCN), which performs end-to-
end learning by integrating spatial graph convolutions with stan-

Zhang [ZLC∗20] proposed STA-GCN, which employs attention

focusing on key spatio-temporal regions. However, the attention
weights themselves may still depend on coupled features, thereby
failing to achieve true independence between spatial and temporal
representations at a fundamental level. Alternatively, other works
aim to enhance the capture of specific spatio-temporal attributes;
for instance, Song [SKK22] focused on improving graph structure
learning, while others designed multi-stream architectures, such as
those incorporating pose and motion streams [ZLC∗20]. Despite
the performance improvements achieved by these methods, they
still lack dedicated mechanisms explicitly designed for effective
spatio-temporal feature learning and optimization.
Existing GCN-based methods also show deficiencies in the ef-
fective fusion of inherent heterogeneous skeleton information. ST-
GCN [LHY∗19] and many subsequent improved models [ZLC∗20,
SKK22,CLY∗21] predominantly adopt a joint-centric strategy, of-
ten neglecting inherent bone geometric attributes and kinematic
information. Even approaches that incorporate supplementary in-
formation sources, such as the palm centroid [NMV23], or utilize
multi-channel networks such as CNN and LSTM combinations,
typically lack dedicated modules designed for extracting bone fea-
tures and effectively fusing them with other heterogeneous data.
Neglecting these types of heterogeneous information restricts the
completeness of skeleton representation and consequently limits
model performance.
In summary, although existing GCN-based gesture recognition
methods show significant progress, two primary bottlenecks per-
sist. First, insufficient spatio-temporal feature learning and opti-
mization limits the fine-grained representation of complex dynam-
ics. Second, the lack of explicit fusion mechanisms for hetero-
geneous information, such as J/B/JM data, leads to incomplete
representations. These factors collectively constrain further model
performance improvements, particularly when handling subtle and
complex gestures. Addressing these issues, this study proposes the
ASTD-Net framework. This framework aims to enhance skeleton-
based gesture recognition accuracy and robustness by promoting
in-depth spatio-temporal feature learning and optimization via the
AMS-GCN Module and the MD-TFN Module, and by achieving
effective fusion of heterogeneous information through the SSTC-A
Module.
3. Method
3.1. Formalization of Skeletal Data and Construction of
Multimodal Representations
The input dynamic 3D hand skeleton sequence is formalized as a
time-evolving graph structure. At any given time step t, the hand
skeleton is represented by its joint coordinates and connectivity.
We denote N = 22 as the total number of joints. The coordinates of
all N joints at time step t are represented by a matrix Jt ∈RN×C,
where C = 3 for 3D coordinates. The i-th row of Jt, denoted as Jt,i,
corresponds to the three-dimensional coordinates (x,y,z) of the i-th
joint.
The connectivity of the skeleton is described by the adjacency
matrix A ∈RN×N, where the matrix element Ai j indicates whether

Consequently, the complete input gesture sequence is repre-

number of frames in the sequence. To comprehensively capture in-
trinsic gesture information from diverse dimensions, three hetero-
geneous modalities are defined based on J.
Modality J directly reflects the absolute spatio-temporal posi-
tions of all joints throughout the entire time series. This modality is
essentially the complete input gesture sequence J, represented as a
tensor J ∈RN×C×T , where C = 3 for 3D coordinates.
Modality B describes the relative bone structure defined by the
skeleton’s connectivity. At each time step t, for any connected pair
of joints (i, j), the corresponding bone vector Bt,(i,j) is computed
as the coordinate difference between the target joint Jt,j and the
source joint Jt,i, Bt,(i,j) = Jt,j −Jt,i. B aggregates these bone vectors
for all N′ =| {(i, j) | Ai j = 1,i ̸= j} | bones across all T frames. Its
dimensions are B ∈RN′×Cb×T where Cb = C = 3.
Modality JM captures the dynamic information concerning joint
position changes over time. Joint velocity is approximated by com-
puting coordinate differences between adjacent frames. For each
time step t ∈{2,...,T} and each joint i, its motion vector JMt,i
is defined as the coordinate difference between the current joint
position Jt,i and the previous frame’s position Jt−1,i, formalized
as JMt,i = Jt,i −Jt−1,i. For t = 1, JM1,i can be set to zero or es-
timated. Modality JM comprises the motion vectors for all joints
across all time frames, resulting in a tensor JM ∈RN×Cm×T where
Cm = C = 3.

3.3. Adaptive Multi-Subgraph Convolution Module
Conventional GCNs, relying on a fixed graph topology, often fail
to capture the intricate and dynamically evolving spatial depen-
dencies in hand dynamics. To address this, we propose the AMS-
GCN Module, as shown in Figure 2. This module integrates struc-
tured spatial graph partitioning with an adaptive adjacency learning
mechanism.

optimized as model parameters, enabling dynamic adjustment of

4. Experiments
The SHREC’17 Track [DSWV∗17] and DHG-14/28 [DSWV16]
datasets comprise data acquired via an Intel RealSense camera op-
erating at 30 frames per second. They contain 2800 gesture se-
quences. Each frame includes a 640× 480 resolution depth im-
age alongside 22 recorded joint coordinates constituting a complete
hand skeleton within both 2D depth image space and 3D world
space. Typical gesture sequences span 20-50 frames. Sequences
are labeled into 14 or 28 classes based on finger count and cate-
gory. Recognition accuracy is evaluated under both 14-class and
28-class settings. Additional parameters for both datasets are de-
tailed in Table 1. The specific hand skeleton structure is illustrated
in Figure 3.

the J and JM modalities the performance of the B modality exhibits

transformations. Conversely the J modality preserves stable spatial
positional information while the JM modality effectively captures
temporal dynamic features enabling them to manifest superior fea-

framework. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed
ASTD-Net method significantly enhances skeleton action recogni-
tion performance while maintaining computational efficiency par-
ticularly yielding prominent improvements for the crucial J and JM
modalities.

5. Conclusion
This study introduces the ASTD-Net to address core challenges
in skeleton gesture recognition, specifically deep spatio-temporal
feature coupling and adaptive heterogeneous information fusion.
Its Adaptive AMS-GCN Module strengthens spatial representa-
tion through optimized graph structure while effectively mitigating
spatial interference via multi-view feature aggregation. Simultane-
ously, the MD-TFN Module leverages parallel multi-kernel/multi-
dilation convolutions to capture multi-granularity temporal dy-
namics. The SSTC-A Module optimizes modal alignment through
temporal discrepancy loss, effectively mitigating information mix-
ing during fusion. ASTD-Net achieves state-of-the-art perfor-
mance with accuracies of 97.50% on SHREC’17 and 93.57%
on DHG-14/28, demonstrating its superior effectiveness. Future
work will focus on developing more lightweight and efficient
spatio-temporal learning architectures to enhance performance in
resource-constrained scenarios.
